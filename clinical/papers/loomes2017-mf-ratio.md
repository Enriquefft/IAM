# Question
What is the true male-to-female ratio for autism spectrum disorder when synthesized systematically across post-DSM-IV/ICD-10 prevalence studies, and is the often-cited 4:1 ratio inflated by diagnostic bias?

# Method
- PRISMA-aligned systematic review and meta-analysis.
- Search of MEDLINE, Embase, PsycINFO; quality rated using risk-of-bias tool.
- Random-effects meta-analysis pooling male-to-female odds ratio (MFOR): odds of being male in ASD vs non-ASD.
- Sensitivity subgroup analysis on high-quality (low risk-of-bias) studies.

# Findings
- Pooled MFOR: 4.20 (95% CI 3.84–4.60). I² = 90.9% (substantial heterogeneity).
- High-quality study subset MFOR: 3.32 (95% CI 2.88–3.84).
- Authors conclude true ratio is closer to 3:1, not 4:1.
- Lower-quality studies (more reliant on clinical referrals) overestimated male predominance.

# Limitations
- High between-study heterogeneity; pooled estimate masks structural variation.
- Even high-quality studies retained referral bias: girls less likely to be brought to assessment.
- No adjustment possible for in-life camouflaging that lowers symptom presentation.
- Ratios derived from prevalence-study era; current ADDM (Shaw 2025: 3.4:1) shows continued narrowing.

# Quotes
> "The true male-to-female ratio is not 4:1, as is often assumed; rather, it is closer to 3:1." — Conclusions
> "Girls who meet criteria for ASD are at disproportionate risk of not receiving a clinical diagnosis." — Conclusions

# Builder takeaways
- Use 3:1 as the working ratio, not 4:1, when sizing female ASD market or designing inclusive products.
- The bias is in detection and referral, not biology — product copy and intake screens that mirror male-coded examples (trains, dinosaurs, social withdrawal) will perpetuate the gap.
- Females meeting criteria but not being diagnosed are an explicit, named subpopulation: this is a product-positioning opportunity for adult-female-focused autism content.
