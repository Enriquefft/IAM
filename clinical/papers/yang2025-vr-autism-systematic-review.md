# Question
Are virtual reality interventions effective for improving social skills in children and adolescents with autism, and which delivery modes (immersive vs nonimmersive) work for which subpopulations?

# Method
- PRISMA-compliant systematic review.
- 14 studies (n=279, ages 3-18).
- Quality assessment with PEDro: 43% high, 29% moderate, 29% low.
- VR delivery split 50/50 between immersive (HMD/CAVE) and nonimmersive (desktop).
- Intervention duration ranged 1 day to 15 weeks.

# Findings
- VR interventions positively affect social skills in children and adolescents with ASD overall.
- High-functioning autism (HFA): significant improvement in social skills, including complex skills.
- Low-functioning autism (LFA): more limited gains, mostly basic life skills.
- Immersive VR superior for complex skills training (HFA).
- Nonimmersive VR more cost-effective and flexible for basic skills (LFA).
- No quantitative meta-analysis was performed (heterogeneity too high).

# Limitations
- Small samples per study.
- Heterogeneous outcome instruments preclude pooled effect.
- Overrepresentation of HFA — generalizability to profound autism limited.
- Adverse events (cybersickness, sensory overload) underreported.
- No long-term follow-up across studies.

# Quotes
> "immersive VR is more suitable for training complex skills in individuals with HFA, while nonimmersive VR stands out in terms of lower cost and flexibility, making it more appropriate for basic skill interventions for people with LFA"

# Builder takeaways
- Evidence supports VR for HFA social skills but is "still in its infancy" for clinical recommendations.
- Hardware barrier in LATAM is severe: Quest 3 (~USD $500) plus content subscriptions exceeds typical disposable budget for autism families in PE/CO/EC.
- Software wedge: 2D web/mobile delivery of structured social-scenario practice (turn-taking, emotion recognition) reproduces nonimmersive VR's evidence base without hardware. The content/protocol is the active ingredient, not the immersion.
- Sensory overload risk is real — any VR-adjacent product needs explicit accommodations (no sudden audio, low-stimulus mode).
