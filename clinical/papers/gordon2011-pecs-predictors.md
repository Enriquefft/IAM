# Question
What changes in communication after PECS training, and which child characteristics predict greater response?

# Method
- Secondary analysis of the Howlin et al. 2007 RCT cohort.
- Outcome: naturalistic classroom observation of communicative acts (picture cards, spontaneous speech, combined).
- Tested baseline child variables (autism severity, expressive language, joint attention) as moderators.

# Findings
- Picture-card use rose post-intervention (Rate Ratio 1.90).
- Spontaneous speech rose modestly (RR 1.77).
- Combined picture+speech communication rose substantially (RR 3.74).
- Spontaneous communication for *requesting objects* increased significantly; social-requesting did not.
- At 10-month follow-up, only the spontaneous speech effect persisted; picture and combined effects faded.
- Less severe baseline autism symptoms predicted larger gains in spontaneous speech.
- Less severe baseline expressive language impairment predicted larger combined-modality gains.
- Children with minimal pre-intervention speech showed the largest *relative* speech gains.

# Limitations
- Same cohort as Howlin 2007 — not independent replication.
- Outcomes measured by classroom observation; observer fidelity reported but inter-site variation possible.
- Predictors are correlational; cannot infer causal moderators.
- 10-month follow-up only.

# Quotes
> "Less severe baseline expressive language impairment ... larger increases in spontaneous use of speech and pictures together." — abstract

# Builder takeaways
- PECS gains are real but specifically for *instrumental requesting* — social-pragmatic functions need different scaffolding (e.g., commenting, sharing).
- Stratify product onboarding by baseline expressive-language level: families with children who have *some* prelinguistic vocalizations are most likely to see speech improvement and so deserve a different success-tracking dashboard.
- Build in 6-month and 12-month nudges; gains decay quickly without continued use.
