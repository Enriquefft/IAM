# Question
Does PECS training produce acquisition of picture exchange, increases in spoken speech, social-communicative gains, and reductions in problem behavior in children with autism?

# Method
- Multiple-baseline-across-participants single-subject design, n=3.
- All 6 phases of PECS taught.
- Measured: phases mastered, mean length of utterance, social-communicative behaviors (eye contact, joint attention, requesting), problem behaviors (tantrums, grabbing, aggression) in play and academic settings.

# Findings
- All 3 children met criterion for PECS phases.
- All 3 showed concomitant increases in spoken vocal speech.
- Increases in social-communicative behaviors (eye contact, joint attention).
- Decreases in problem behavior across all 3 children.

# Limitations
- n=3, single setting, no randomization.
- Frequently cited as foundational evidence but not generalizable on its own.
- Therapist-implemented; ecological validity to home/school undocumented.
- Speech-emergence findings later moderated by larger studies (see [howlin2007-pecs-rct], [flippin2010-pecs-meta]).

# Quotes
> "All 3 children met the learning criterion for PECS and showed concomitant increases in verbal speech." — results

# Builder takeaways
- This paper drives much of the parent-marketing claim that "PECS leads to speech." Subsequent meta-analyses do not confirm the speech effect at population scale — be cautious about replicating this claim in marketing.
- Reduction in problem behavior is the more replicable secondary outcome and a defensible product benefit when paired with functional-communication scaffolding.
