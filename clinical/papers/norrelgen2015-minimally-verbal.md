# Question
What proportion of preschool-age children with ASD remain minimally verbal after community-based early intervention?

# Method
- Cohort of 165 children with ASD, ages 4–6.
- Vineland Adaptive Behavior Scales used for language assessment.
- Operational definition: minimally verbal if expressive language age <24 months or use of <3 spoken words / no two-word phrases.

# Findings
- 15% of sample classified as nonverbal (fewer than 3 spoken words).
- 10% additional minimally verbal (some words but limited phrase speech).
- Combined nonverbal + minimally verbal: 25%.
- In later preschool-age samples (mean ages 59–66 months), 25–44% continue minimally verbal.

# Limitations
- Single-country (Sweden) cohort.
- Community-intervention setting — outcomes vary by intervention quality.
- Cross-sectional snapshot of preschool ages, not longitudinal.
- Excludes children outside the early-intervention system.

# Quotes
> 25% combined nonverbal/minimally verbal by ages 4–6 in a community early-intervention sample. — Results

# Builder takeaways
- Preschool-age 25% minimally-verbal figure is consistent with school-age 30% (Tager-Flusberg & Kasari 2013) — the rate stabilizes early.
- AAC introduction by ages 4–6 is the evidence-aligned window — earlier-onboarding products win the long-term user.
- For LATAM where formal early intervention is less universal, the minimally-verbal proportion at school entry is likely higher than the 25–30% canonical range.
