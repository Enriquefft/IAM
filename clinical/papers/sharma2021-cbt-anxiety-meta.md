# Question
What is the efficacy of adapted CBT for reducing anxiety symptoms in children and adolescents on the autism spectrum?

# Method
- Meta-analysis of 19 RCTs (487 CBT, 346 control), 833 total participants.
- Adapted CBT protocols: BIACA, Coping Cat, Cool Kids, Exploring Feelings.
- Outcomes: anxiety symptom rated by clinician, parent, child.
- Hedges' g, random-effects.

# Findings
- Clinician-rated anxiety: g = 0.88 (95% CI 0.55 to 1.12), large effect, k=11.
- Parent-rated anxiety: g = 0.40 (95% CI 0.24 to 0.56), small-medium, k=18.
- Child-rated anxiety: g = 0.25 (95% CI 0.06 to 0.43), small, k=13.
- Risk of bias: 63% rated "some concerns" (mostly reporting bias; only 37% pre-registered).
- Follow-up data sparse; sustained benefits unclear.

# Limitations
- Strong informant discrepancy — clinician-rated effects 3× child-rated.
- Few studies report follow-up.
- All pre-registration concerns; publication bias not formally tested.
- Most studies on cognitively able autistic youth (IQ > 70).

# Quotes
> "Substantial inconsistency emerged in the magnitude of benefit depending upon who was rating symptoms."

# Builder takeaways
- CBT for anxiety is the strongest mental-health intervention in the autism literature — large clinician-rated effects.
- Software opportunity: adapted CBT modules for autistic youth (visual schedules, concrete language, special-interest-driven examples) — low-IQ populations under-studied.
- KPI design: report parent-rated and child-rated outcomes, not only clinician — more honest and aligns with the discrepancy literature.
- Expand: very limited data on adapted CBT for depression in autistic populations — research gap and product opportunity.
