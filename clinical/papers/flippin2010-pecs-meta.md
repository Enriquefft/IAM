# Question
Does the Picture Exchange Communication System (PECS) produce gains in functional communication and in spoken speech for children with ASD?

# Method
- Systematic review + meta-analysis of PECS literature published 1994–June 2009.
- Inclusion: studies on PECS for children diagnosed with ASD.
- 11 studies: 8 single-subject experiments (n=18) and 3 group studies (95 PECS, 65 controls).
- Primary outcomes: (a) functional communication / picture exchange acquisition, (b) spoken speech.

# Findings
- Small-to-moderate gains in functional communication following PECS training.
- Speech outcomes were small to negative across studies.
- PECS classified as "promising but not yet established" evidence-based intervention at time of review.
- Maintenance and generalization were repeatedly flagged as weak.
- Phase IV (sentence structure phase) tentatively associated with stronger speech outcomes when reached, but few studies reported reaching Phase IV.

# Limitations
- Heterogeneous designs (mostly single-subject).
- Few group RCTs at time of review.
- Effect-size aggregation limited by study reporting.
- Maintenance often not measured beyond 1 month.
- Speech outcome operationally varied across studies.

# Quotes
> "Small to moderate gains in communication were demonstrated following training. Gains in speech were small to negative." — abstract

# Builder takeaways
- Symbol-exchange protocols can be expected to produce reliable gains in *requesting* but not in spoken speech.
- A wellness/education product built on picture exchange should target communication acts and family routines, not advertise speech outcomes.
- Phase IV scaffolding (sentence strips) is the under-studied phase — opportunity to ship structured Phase IV practice.
