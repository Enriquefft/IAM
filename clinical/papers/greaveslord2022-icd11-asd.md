# Question
What are the innovations of the ICD-11 (effective January 2022) for autism spectrum disorder, and how do they differ from DSM-5?

# Method
- Narrative review integrating ICD-11 diagnostic criteria with biopsychosocial and neurodiversity perspectives.

# Findings
- ICD-11 includes atypical responses to sensory stimuli as a core diagnostic feature for ASD; ICD-10 did not.
- ICD-11 explicitly recognizes that some autistic individuals first experience overt social challenges only when societal demands increase (e.g., adolescence/adulthood) — flexibility around early-onset criterion.
- ICD-11 does not require a fixed count of features; clinical judgment integrates persistent social-communication deficits with restricted, repetitive, inflexible patterns plus functional impairment.
- ICD-11 qualifiers replace DSM-5 severity levels:
  - Intellectual development qualifier: Mild/Moderate/Severe/Profound/Provisional.
  - Functional language qualifier: (i) mild or no impairment; (ii) impaired (single words/simple phrases); (iii) complete or near-complete absence.
- ICD-11 does not list certain DSM-5 examples (flipping objects, excessive smelling/touching, echolalia, stimming) as required exemplars — these are framed as variable presentations rather than ID-coupled features.
- ICD-11 recognizes regression: "Loss of previously acquired skills is rarely (spontaneously) observed after 3 years of age."
- ICD-11 avoids a separate Social (Pragmatic) Communication Disorder diagnosis; functional language impairment is encoded via qualifier on the main ASD diagnosis.
- ICD-11 explicitly accommodates adult/adolescent first presentations through life-course interview methodology.

# Limitations
- Narrative review; no empirical comparison of inter-system diagnostic agreement.
- Does not quantify how many DSM-5 ASD cases would meet ICD-11 criteria (or vice versa).

# Quotes
> "atypical responses to sensory stimuli are included as part of the diagnostic requirements in ICD-11, in contrast to ICD-10, where unusual sensory processing was not yet considered a core (diagnostic) feature" — Current ICD-11 Definition section

# Builder takeaways
- For LATAM products: ICD-11 is the operative billing/clinical taxonomy in Peru, Mexico, Colombia, Chile (WHO-aligned national health systems). DSM-5-TR is the research/private-sector reference.
- ICD-11's language qualifier (3 levels) is more useful for AAC product segmentation than DSM-5's three support levels — segments map directly onto AAC user needs (low/some/no functional language).
- ICD-11's recognition of late-presenting autism justifies adult-facing product variants and content.
- Product copy targeting clinicians in LATAM should use ICD-11 terminology; product copy targeting US payers/parents should use DSM-5-TR.
