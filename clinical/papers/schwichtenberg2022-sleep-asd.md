# Question
What is the prevalence and pattern of sleep problems in children with autism spectrum disorder, and how do these compare to typically developing peers?

# Method
- Narrative review with systematic update of empirical studies 2018–2021.
- 60 studies, n=24,229 children.

# Findings
- Across questionnaire studies (n=17), prevalence of any sleep problem: M=71.4% (SD 15.9; range 37–93%).
- Historical estimate: 40–83% of autistic individuals have sleep disturbance.
- 27 of 60 studies (45%) report less optimal sleep behaviors in autism vs comparison groups.
- Most common sleep issues: sleep onset insomnia ~65%; night waking 47.7%; parasomnias 46.8%; obstructive sleep apnea ~23%.
- Decreased sleep pressure observed in autistic children.
- Categories: insomnias, parasomnias, sleep-disordered breathing, circadian rhythm disorders, daytime sleepiness, sleep movement disorders.

# Limitations
- Narrative + systematic mixed methodology.
- High prevalence range (37–93%) reflects measurement heterogeneity.
- Most studies parent-report, not polysomnography.

# Builder takeaways
- Use ~70% as working figure for sleep-problem prevalence in autistic children — supports product features around bedtime routines, melatonin guidance, sleep hygiene visuals.
- Sleep onset insomnia (~65%) is the dominant subtype — highest-leverage intervention target.
- 23% OSA prevalence triples general-population child rate; products that screen for snoring/breathing pauses can refer appropriately.
