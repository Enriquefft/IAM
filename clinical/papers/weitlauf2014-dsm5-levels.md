# Question
Do DSM-5 severity (support) levels for autism — based on autism symptoms — align with cognitive and adaptive functioning impairment levels assessed independently?

# Method
- Descriptive cross-sectional analysis.
- Compared severity classifications across three measures: autism symptoms, cognitive skills, adaptive functioning.
- Sample: 726 children with ASD from clinical registry.

# Findings
- Only 24.7% of participants classified as "severe" on all three measures simultaneously.
- Only 3.4% classified as "moderate" across all three.
- Only 0.3% classified as "mild" across all three.
- Most participants scored high (severe) on autism symptoms but spread across mild/moderate/severe ranges on cognitive and adaptive measures.
- DSM-5 does not specify quantitative method for assigning severity levels — no operational definition.

# Limitations
- Single-site clinical registry; over-represents complex cases.
- Pre-DSM-5 publication, draft DSM-5 used.
- Did not measure provider variability in severity assignment.

# Quotes
> "Although qualitative differences between support levels are described, quantitative methods or practice recommendations for differentiating between levels remain undetermined." — Background
> "without an established method, it will be difficult to interpret what these support modifiers mean when they are assigned according to site- and provider-specific criteria." — Discussion

# Builder takeaways
- DSM-5 Levels 1/2/3 are clinical heuristics, not standardized scales — products should not depend on level alone for personalization.
- A user labeled "Level 1" may have severe adaptive deficits; a user labeled "Level 3" may have intact cognition. Personalization must read functional dimensions (communication, sensory, motor, executive function) directly, not from the level label.
- Marketing copy that segments by "Level 1/2/3" misrepresents the construct — better to segment by support type/intensity.
