# Question
Does AAC intervention impede, leave unchanged, or improve spoken speech production specifically in children with autism / PDD-NOS?

# Method
- Systematic review of literature 1975–May 2007.
- Multifaceted database search (PsycInfo, MEDLINE, ERIC, hand search of AAC journals, dissertations, ancestry searching).
- 11 studies included: 9 single-subject studies (27 participants), 2 group studies (98 participants).
- Outcome: change in vocal speech with AAC.

# Findings
- AAC interventions did not impede speech production in any study.
- Most studies reported speech increases.
- Gains were "rather modest" in magnitude.
- Single-subject studies showed greater detail on individual variability.
- Authors call for prediction models keyed to baseline imitation and object exploration.

# Limitations
- Heterogeneous outcomes across studies (vocalization count vs. word imitations vs. spontaneous speech).
- Mostly underpowered single-subject designs.
- Pre-iPad era.
- "Modest" gains not quantified to common effect-size metric.

# Quotes
> "AAC interventions do not appear to impede speech production and may result in increased speech production, [but] the modest gains observed require realistic expectations among clinicians and other stakeholders." — abstract

# Builder takeaways
- For autism specifically (narrower than [millar2006-aac-speech]), evidence still says no harm and some benefit to speech.
- Avoid overpromising. Set parent expectations to "modest" speech improvement, with the primary win being functional communication acts.
- Pair AAC scaffolding with prelinguistic skill-building (imitation, object play) — the populations most likely to see speech gains.
