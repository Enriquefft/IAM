# Question
What are the effects of ABA-based interventions (ABA, DTT, PECS, ESDM) on autism core symptoms, language, socialization, communication, IQ, adaptive behavior, and repetitive behaviors compared to conventional intervention controls?

# Method
- Meta-analysis of 14 RCTs (278 experimental, 277 control).
- Inclusion: children 0–18 with ASD; ABA-based interventions; standardized outcome measures.
- Outcomes: ADOS general symptoms, socialization, communication, expressive/receptive language, adaptive behavior, IQ (verbal, nonverbal), repetitive behaviors, motor, cognition.
- Random-effects model, standardized mean differences.
- Risk-of-bias: detection bias generally low (clinician-rated scales blinded); blinding of participants impossible.

# Findings
- General autism symptoms: d = -0.36 (95% CI -1.31 to 0.58), p = 0.45, NS. I² = 94%.
- Socialization: d = 0.11 (95% CI -0.31 to 0.54), p = 0.60, NS.
- Communication: d = 0.30 (95% CI -0.02 to 0.61), p = 0.07, trend.
- Expressive language: d = -3.52 (95% CI -6.31 to -0.72), p = 0.01, significant (large but high heterogeneity).
- Receptive language: d = -0.04 (95% CI -0.44 to 0.36), p = 0.84, NS.
- IQ, verbal IQ, nonverbal IQ, adaptive behavior, daily living, motor, cognition, repetitive behaviors: NS.

# Limitations
- Small number of RCTs prevented separate analyses by intervention type.
- High heterogeneity for general symptoms (I²=94%) and expressive language.
- Comparator was "conventional intervention," not no-treatment — undercounts effect vs untreated.
- Blinding of participants/parents impossible.

# Quotes
> "outcomes of socialization, communication and expressive language may be promising targets for ABA-based interventions" — Authors' conclusion

> "However, significant effects for the outcomes of autism general symptoms, receptive language, adaptive behavior, daily living skills, IQ, verbal IQ, nonverbal IQ, restricted and repetitive behavior, motor and cognition were not observed."

# Builder takeaways
- Even pooled across 14 RCTs, ABA's only statistically significant effect was on expressive language; other outcomes NS or NS-trend.
- Findings undercut the marketing claim that ABA broadly "treats autism" — it teaches discrete skills.
- For a wellness/education product, framing around skill-building (vocabulary, requesting) has empirical support; framing around symptom reduction does not.
