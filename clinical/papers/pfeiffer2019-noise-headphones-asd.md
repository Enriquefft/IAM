# Question
Do noise-attenuating headphones (Bose QuietComfort 15 over-ear and QuietComfort 20i in-ear) reduce physiological arousal in children with ASD and auditory hypersensitivity in their natural environment?

# Method
- Single-subject multi-treatment design (ABAC randomized).
- 4 phases per child over 4 weeks; 16-20 data collection occasions per child.
- Outcomes: skin conductance level (SCL), non-specific skin conductance responses (NS-SCR), measured via Empatica E4.
- Environmental decibel readings collected.

# Findings
- Both over-ear and in-ear noise-attenuating headphones significantly reduced SCL and NS-SCR frequency from baseline (p < 0.001).
- Headphones buffered physiological arousal as ambient noise rose.
- 88% usable wearable data quality.

# Effect sizes
| Outcome | Measure | Effect | 95% CI | p |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| SCL reduction | autonomic arousal | significant decrease | n/r | <0.001 |
| NS-SCR frequency | autonomic arousal | significant decrease | n/r | <0.001 |

# Limitations
- n=6 with 50% attrition.
- No behavioral/functional outcomes (only physiology).
- Single-subject design constrains causal inference.
- "Aware mode" use of in-ear headphones not tracked.

# Quotes
> "the use of noise attenuating headphones for individuals with ASD and hyperacusis may reduce sympathetic activation"

# Builder takeaways
- Best-available physiological evidence for noise-canceling headphones in autism — but very weak design.
- Bose QC consumer prices (~USD $200-400) are the realistic cost; LATAM availability is acceptable through grey-market import.
- Loop Quiet (~USD $25, NRR 14 dB) is dramatically cheaper passive alternative — no published autism-specific RCT but used widely.
- Wedge: software cannot substitute for noise reduction itself, but a sensory-environment-mapping tool (rate venues, predict trigger spaces) is high-value adjunct that all hardware misses.
