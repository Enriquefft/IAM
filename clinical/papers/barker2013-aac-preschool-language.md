# Question
Which classroom AAC-related practices (teacher prompting, peer use of AAC, augmented input) predict language growth in preschoolers with developmental disabilities?

# Method
- Longitudinal correlational study, ~2 years.
- 71 preschool children with developmental disabilities; AAC users.
- Teacher surveys describing classroom AAC practices.
- Outcome measures: Mullen Scales of Early Learning (Receptive, Expressive), PPVT-4, Number of Different Words.
- Multiple regression with classroom variables predicting growth, controlling for baseline.

# Findings
- Peer AAC use predicted *stronger* receptive language and expressive vocabulary growth.
- Teacher prompting was *negatively* correlated with growth on all measures (likely reflects child severity, not causal harm).
- Augmented input by adults was inconsistently predictive in this sample.

# Effect sizes
| Outcome | Measure | Effect | 95% CI | p |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Receptive (MSEL) | β peer-AAC | 0.23 | n/a | .03 |
| PPVT-4 | β peer-AAC | 0.25 | n/a | .01 |
| Number of different words | β peer-AAC | 0.24 | n/a | .03 |
| MSEL Receptive | β teacher-prompting | -0.28 | n/a | <.01 |
| PPVT-4 | β teacher-prompting | -0.32 | n/a | <.01 |
| MSEL Expressive | β teacher-prompting | -0.27 | n/a | <.01 |

# Limitations
- Correlational; no random assignment.
- Teacher prompting effect likely confounded by baseline child need.
- Survey data; no observational fidelity check.
- Single US site.

# Quotes
> "Peer use of AAC to provide augmented input was associated with stronger language growth." — discussion

# Builder takeaways
- Peer modeling is the strongest classroom predictor — opportunity to design product features that enable *neurotypical peers* to engage with the AAC system (joint games, shared boards).
- Adult prompting alone is not sufficient — implies in-app modeling and peer-pairing UX outperform "more reminders to parent."
- For schools as a distribution channel: a product that supports inclusive classrooms (peer-friendly UX, multi-user mode) aligns with the strongest evidence-driver of language growth.
