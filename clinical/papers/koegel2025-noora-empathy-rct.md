# Question
Does a GPT-4-driven AI chatbot ("Noora") delivering real-time feedback on empathic statements improve empathic responses in autistic adolescents/adults vs waitlist control, and do gains generalize to social conversation?

# Method
- Randomized clinical trial; experimental vs waitlist control; 4-week intervention.
- Noora: GPT-4 chatbot delivering structured empathic-response trials with real-time feedback. Built on prior face-to-face empathy training paradigm (Koegel lab).
- Experimental group: 10 trials/day, 5 days/week × 4 weeks = 200 trials total.
- Comparison: empathic response counts, baseline vs final 50 responses, and generalization probes in natural conversation.

# Findings
- 71% of experimental group improved empathic-response count.
- Average +13.2 percentage-point gain comparing first 50 to last 50 responses (per scoping review citation).
- Statistically significant improvement vs waitlist on empathic responses (p reported as significant; exact value not extracted).
- Improvement generalized to natural social conversation (independent rater–scored).
- Participants reported increased confidence and high satisfaction.

# Limitations
- n=30; pilot-scale RCT.
- Authors include the Noora development team (Koegel lab + Lam lab, Stanford) — conflict of interest.
- Waitlist control does not isolate AI-vs-human-coach component.
- "Occasional discrepancies between AI and human raters" acknowledged.
- 4-week intervention; no long-term retention or maintenance data published.
- Population recruited around Stanford; limited representativeness.
- Adolescent/adult sample (11–35); does not generalize to younger children.
- Single LLM (GPT-4); no head-to-head model comparison.

# Builder takeaways
- The ONLY published RCT on an LLM-based chatbot for autistic users (as of May 2026). Cite as such — treat as low-confidence preliminary evidence, not established practice.
- Targets a narrow skill (empathic statement formation) with clear input/output structure — exactly the kind of tightly-bounded use case where LLMs work and can be evaluated.
- Adolescent+ population only. No data for young children, where LLM deployment risks (sycophancy, safety) are highest.
- The intervention's effect comes from structured practice + feedback, not free-form chat. Wellness/education products should mirror this: structured trials, not open conversation.
- Author conflict of interest is real — replication by an independent lab would substantially raise confidence.
