# Question
What is the documented history of autism diagnosis, services, advocacy, and policy in Peru from the early 20th century to 2022, and what gaps remain?

# Method
- Documentary research; searches in Google Scholar, Victor Larco Herrera Hospital library, Universidad Peruana Cayetano Heredia library, January–July 2022.
- No new empirical data; synthesis of historical sources, policies, and registry data.

# Findings
- 1915: Hermilio Valdizán initiated scientific study of nervous/mental illness in Peru.
- 1944: First documented "infantile schizophrenia" case (Luis Guerra).
- 1959: Emilio Majluf presented first autism case (categorized as "infantile schizomorphic syndrome").
- 1979: Centro Ann Sullivan founded by Lily Mayo, introducing ABA and functional-natural curriculum.
- 1980: DSM-III "infantile autism" entry catalyzed Peruvian diagnostic uptake.
- 1993: PECS introduced in Peru.
- 1999: ASPAU-Peru (parent advocacy association) founded.
- 2014: Law 30150 enacted for ASD protection.
- 2019: First National Plan for Autism developed.
- National Registry data: 4 ASD registrations in 2001 → 836 in 2011 → 8,835 in 2021 (>2,000-fold increase over two decades).
- No epidemiological prevalence study has been conducted in Peru.
- Specialized services concentrated in Lima; private centers focus on children/adolescents, leaving adults under-served.
- Persistent shortage of trained professionals (per MINSA and EsSalud).

# Limitations
- Historical narrative; not population data.
- Registry counts reflect identification capacity, not actual prevalence.
- No regional (non-Lima) primary data.

# Builder takeaways
- Peru has zero population-based autism prevalence studies — any number quoted for Peru is extrapolated from registry counts or LATAM/Chile estimates.
- Two-decade registry growth (4 → 8,835) is a service-availability story, not an epidemiology story; useful for showing demand growth.
- Service concentration in Lima defines the geographic primary market. Outside-Lima population is the underserved frontier.
- Adult ASD population is essentially absent from formal services — opportunity for adult-facing wellness/education products.
- ASPAU-Peru and Centro Ann Sullivan are the primary established advocacy/service institutions; potential partnership/distribution targets.
