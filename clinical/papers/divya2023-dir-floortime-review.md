# Question
What are the developmental outcomes of DIR/Floortime intervention for children with autism, and what is the strength of supporting evidence?

# Method
- Systematic review of 12 studies (2010–2020).
- Designs: 2 prospective follow-ups, 1 RCT, 1 cross-sectional, 1 correlational, 2 case studies, 1 quasi-experimental, 1 mixed-methods, 1 longitudinal evaluation, 1 single-subject, 1 pretest-posttest control.
- Outcomes: emotional/social development, communication, daily living, parent-child interaction.

# Findings
- Substantial reported gains in emotive functioning and social/relationship skills across studies.
- Significant gains in communication and parent-child interaction quality.
- Enhanced adaptive behavior and self-care in some studies.
- No adverse events reported.
- Authors note effect sizes not formally reported; varied outcome measures preventing pooled estimates.
- Only one true RCT in the set.

# Limitations
- Only English-language studies.
- Small samples; heterogeneous durations.
- Primarily home-based with parent self-report (high bias risk).
- Limited socioeconomic and geographic diversity.
- Single RCT in 12-study pool.

# Quotes
> "Home-based floortime improved emotive functioning, communication, and daily living skills"

> "Floortime is a cost-effective, completely child-led approach, which could be initiated as early as possible"

# Builder takeaways
- Evidence base for DIR/Floortime is "emerging" — promising directional signal but no anchoring meta-analysis with effect sizes.
- The model's child-led, affect-driven engagement style has high parent-acceptability and aligns with neurodiversity values.
- Software opportunity: parent coaching tools that prompt joint-attention bids, follow-the-child rhythms — an underbuilt category.
