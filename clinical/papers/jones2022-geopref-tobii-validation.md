# Question
Does the GeoPref test (eye-tracking attention to social vs geometric scenes) reliably identify an autism subtype at large scale in a primary care screening setting?

# Method
- Tobii T120 eye tracker (60 Hz, 1280x1024).
- 62-second dynamic geometric vs social images stimulus.
- 1,863 toddlers across community and screening referral pathways.

# Findings
- Test-retest reliability >85%.
- Positive predictive value 86-100%.
- Specificity 98%.
- Sensitivity ~20% (consistently low — biomarker identifies a specific subtype).
- Strong-non-social-preference toddlers had higher symptom severity, weaker language/cognitive ability, weaker social-visual brain network connectivity, worse school-age outcomes.

# Limitations
- Low sensitivity means biomarker is rule-in not rule-out.
- US single research center.
- Hardware-bound (Tobii eye tracker, controlled lighting).

# Builder takeaways
- High specificity / low sensitivity is the canonical pattern for biomarker subtype detection — useful as triage to confirm referral, useless as universal screen.
- For LATAM: a webcam-based reproduction would lose precision; not a substitute. The clinical value is when an autism subtype is identified, prognosis information is actionable.
