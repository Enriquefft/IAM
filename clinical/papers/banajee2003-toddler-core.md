# Question
What core vocabulary do typically developing toddlers (24–36 months) use most frequently in everyday preschool routines, and what list should inform AAC vocabulary selection for similar-age peers with complex communication needs?

# Method
- Naturalistic language sampling from 50 typically developing toddlers across 5 preschools.
- Two activity contexts sampled: free play in interest centers, and snack time.
- Vocabulary frequency tallied across both contexts.
- Word lists ranked by frequency and proportion of children using each word.

# Findings
- 23 words accounted for ~96% of all spoken words used by toddlers across both routines.
- The 23 words: I, no, yes/yeah, my, the, want, is, it, that, a, go, mine, you, what, on, in, here, more, out, off, some, help, all done/finished.
- Word classes: pronouns, verbs, prepositions, demonstratives — almost no nouns in the core.
- 9 words used by all 50 toddlers across both routines.

# Limitations
- US English, single regional sample.
- Two activity contexts only; vocabulary in other settings (home, doctor visits, mealtimes at home) not sampled.
- Typically developing toddlers — not children with complex communication needs.
- Frequency lists do not capture *which* words drive successful communication acts.

# Quotes

# Builder takeaways
- The 23-word core list is the de-facto starting vocabulary for early-childhood AAC apps. Boards built around these words enable a high % of communicative coverage with minimal screen real estate.
- Critical: this list is almost all function words and verbs — *not* nouns. AAC apps that lead with food/animal/toy nouns are mismatched to actual toddler usage.
- Spanish-language equivalent does not have an analogous published list. Building one for LATAM is a defensible product/research contribution.
