# Question
Does Ayres Sensory Integration (ASI) intervention, when delivered with fidelity, produce evidence-based outcomes for children with autism per Council for Exceptional Children (CEC) standards?

# Method
- Systematic review using CEC quality indicators.
- 3 studies met inclusion: 2 RCTs (Pfeiffer 2011, Schaaf 2014; Level 1b) and 1 non-RCT (Iwanaga 2014).
- ASI fidelity required (per Parham et al fidelity measure).
- Outcomes: Goal Attainment Scaling (GAS), behaviors of concern, motor, social.

# Findings
- Schaaf 2014: GAS Cohen's d = 1.20; caregiver assistance for self-care d = 0.9; social activities d = 0.7.
- Pfeiffer 2011: GAS d 0.360 (teacher) and 0.125 (parent); autism mannerisms d = 0.131.
- Iwanaga 2014: average d = 0.23 (below 0.25 threshold).
- Schaaf met 100% CEC criteria; Pfeiffer 85%; Iwanaga ~50%.
- Authors conclude ASI "meets criteria for an evidence-based practice" for children 4-12 with autism.

# Limitations
- Only 3 studies, combined n=96.
- Limited to children with IQ >65 (most studies).
- Heterogeneous dose (18-40 sessions).
- Outcome was Goal Attainment Scaling — by design, calibrated to expectation; vulnerable to inflation.
- Note: previous reviews using "sensory-based" interventions (broader category) reached opposite conclusions.

# Quotes
> "ASI meets criteria for an evidence-based practice for children with autism ages 4-12 years old."

# Builder takeaways
- Strongest endorsement ASI gets in the literature — but only with fidelity to Ayres protocol and only on individualized goals (GAS).
- Does NOT support claims about reducing autism core symptoms or behaviors of concern.
- Software replacing/augmenting ASI: nearly impossible — ASI requires hands-on equipment (suspended swings, weighted vestibular gear). Augmentation rather than replacement.
