# Question
From the perspective of speech-language pathologists, what factors discriminate AAC systems that achieve long-term use from those that are inappropriately abandoned?

# Method
- Three-phase mixed-methods design.
- Phase 1: 6 focus groups identified candidate factors.
- Phase 2: 106-item survey developed.
- Phase 3: 275 ASHA SIG-12 members completed survey.
- Factor analysis on responses.

# Findings
- Mean of 39.35% of AAC systems still in use after one year (i.e., ~60% abandonment rate as reported by SLPs in their caseload).
- Constructs predicting *success*: Support, Attitude, System characteristics and Fit.
- Constructs predicting *abandonment*: Not Maintaining/Adjusting the System, Attitude, Lack of Training, Lack of Support, Poor Fit.

# Limitations
- SLP self-report; caseload-weighted, not user-weighted.
- US ASHA-member sample only.
- 2006 — pre-iPad; modern app-based AAC may have different abandonment dynamics.
- Survey responses may underestimate abandonment if SLPs lose contact with abandoned cases.

# Quotes
> "The mean percentage of people who used AAC for more than a year after being introduced by speech-language pathologists was only 39.35%." — results

# Builder takeaways
- The "60% abandoned within a year" claim widely cited by AAC vendors traces back to this paper. Use it as the benchmark to beat.
- "Failure to maintain/adjust the system" is the modifiable lever — products that auto-suggest vocabulary additions, retire unused symbols, and prompt board updates address the #1 abandonment driver.
- Support and training matter as much as system design — bundle in-app training, quick-start coaching videos, and live support escalation.
