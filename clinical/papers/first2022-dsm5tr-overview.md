# Question
What changed in the DSM-5-TR (March 2022) relative to DSM-5 (2013), specifically for autism spectrum disorder?

# Method
- Editorial overview by APA's DSM revision leadership.
- Catalogues all text revisions and criterion-set changes across DSM-5-TR.

# Findings
- DSM-5-TR published 18 March 2022.
- Autism Criterion A wording revised from "as manifested by the following" to "as manifested by all of the following" — clarification, not a substantive change.
- Stated rationale: prevent misreading that allowed clinicians to treat the three social communication deficit types (A1, A2, A3) as a menu rather than required-all.
- "All of the following" preserves the original DSM-5 intent and "high diagnostic threshold."
- Specifier wording for co-occurring conditions broadened from "another neurodevelopmental, mental, or behavioral disorder" to "...problem" — captures relevant difficulties that fall short of disorder thresholds.
- APA stated neither change is expected to materially alter the number of people diagnosed.
- Other autism-related diagnostic categories (Social Communication Disorder, Stereotypic Movement Disorder, Intellectual Disability) received parallel text clarifications, no criterion changes.

# Limitations
- Editorial perspective; no empirical evaluation of impact.
- "No expected impact on diagnoses" is an assertion, not measured.

# Quotes
> "criterion A was revised to be clearer: 'as manifested by all of the following'" — autism criterion section
> The revision aims to "maintain a high diagnostic threshold by requiring all three" social communication deficit types. — autism criterion section

# Builder takeaways
- For product copy and onboarding, use DSM-5-TR (current) terminology, not DSM-5.
- The "all three" social communication requirement is a real diagnostic gate — products targeting "subclinical" or "broader autism phenotype" populations should not claim to address ASD without acknowledging this threshold.
- The expanded "problem" specifier opens product positioning around co-occurring difficulties (anxiety, sensory regulation) that the user does not have a separate diagnosis for.
