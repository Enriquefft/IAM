# Question
Does adding a NAO humanoid robot as co-therapist to a Theory-of-Mind + ABA-based intervention outperform an identical human-only intervention for autistic children?

# Method
- RCT with baseline, post-intervention, 3-month follow-up.
- 21 sessions over 3 months (2/week, 35–45 min).
- NAO operated semi-autonomously delivering verbal/nonverbal communication and gesture.
- Outcomes: CARS-2, NEPSY-II (Affect Recognition, Theory of Mind, Inhibition), CBCL (Internalizing/Externalizing), SDQ Prosocial.
- Comparator: identical content delivered by human therapist alone.

# Findings
- Both groups improved significantly.
- CARS-2 change <4.5-point clinical-significance threshold in both groups.
- NEPSY-II Affect Recognition and ToM: significant gains in both groups (p<.01).
- NEPSY-II Inhibition: fewer errors and faster completion (p<.05).
- CBCL Internalizing/Externalizing: significant reduction in both (p<.05).
- SDQ Prosocial: significant increase in both (p<.05).
- Between-group comparison: minimal statistically significant differences. Robot group reported higher satisfaction. No clinically meaningful superiority for the robot.

# Limitations
- Limited follow-up data (only n=22 at 3-month).
- COVID-19 disruption.
- Teacher-reported data compromised by online instruction.
- Robot recording technical failures prevented full data analysis.
- Concurrent therapies confounded results.
- No untreated control — both arms got intensive intervention.

# Quotes
> CARS-2 change "was less than the proposed 4.5 points threshold" for clinical efficacy. — Results

# Builder takeaways
- When robot is properly compared to human-delivered identical content, the robot does not outperform — it adds satisfaction/novelty but not measurable clinical superiority.
- "Robot helps autistic kid" headlines almost never include the human-control arm. When they do, the effect collapses.
- For wellness/education products: robotic embodiment is not a feature you can claim drives outcomes; it drives engagement/satisfaction. Frame accordingly.
- ABA-derived content was used here without modification — this paper does not address neurodiversity-led concerns about ABA suppression of autistic behaviors.
