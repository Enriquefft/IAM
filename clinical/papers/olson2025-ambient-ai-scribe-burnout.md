# Question
Does an ambient AI scribe (Abridge) reduce clinician burnout and administrative burden after 30 days of use?

# Method
- Quality-improvement pre/post survey, no control arm.
- 6 US health systems, Feb–Oct 2024.
- Intervention: Abridge ambient AI scribe; clinicians recorded encounters; system generated notes for review/editing before EHR commit.

# Findings
- Burnout: 51.9% → 38.8% (OR 0.26; 95% CI 0.13–0.54; p<.001) — 74% reduction in burnout odds.
- Cognitive task load (10-point): -2.64 (p<.001).
- After-hours documentation: -0.90 hours/week (p<.001).
- Patient attention focus: +2.05 (p<.001).
- Patient comprehension of care plans: +0.44 (p=.005).

# Limitations
- No control group for temporal trends.
- Convenience sample with self-selection bias toward technology adopters.
- Non-anonymous surveys.
- Subjective measures; no EHR-based time validation.
- One academic center (n=69) excluded from burnout analysis.
- General ambulatory clinicians, not pediatric SLP/OT specifically.

# Builder takeaways
- Largest signal yet for AI scribes reducing burnout, but no control arm — effect inflated by novelty + selection bias.
- Notes-generation alone yields ~1 hour/week reduction in after-hours work. Marginal but real.
- For SLP/OT/psych pediatric work, this evidence does not transfer directly — pediatric note structure (parent narrative, behavioral observation, IEP integration) is different from adult ambulatory.
- Attention-on-patient improvement is the marketable claim that resonates with both clinicians and parents.
