# Question
Does a weighted blanket increase total sleep time in autistic children with severe sleep problems compared to an identical-weight control blanket?

# Method
- Randomized placebo-controlled crossover trial (Phase III).
- 73 randomized, 67 completed; 2 weeks per arm with crossover.
- Primary outcome: total sleep time via actigraphy.
- Secondary: sleep-onset latency, efficiency, behavioral assessment, family functioning, adverse events, parent diaries.

# Findings
- Weighted blanket did not increase total sleep time vs control (no significant difference on actigraphy).
- No differences in sleep-onset latency, sleep efficiency, or behavioral outcomes.
- Parents and children subjectively preferred weighted blanket.

# Effect sizes
| Outcome | Measure | Effect | 95% CI | p |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Total sleep time | actigraphy minutes | no difference | n/r | n.s. |
| Sleep onset latency | actigraphy minutes | no difference | n/r | n.s. |

# Limitations
- 2-week crossover may be too short to detect adaptation.
- Severe-sleep-problem inclusion criterion limits generalizability to milder cases.
- Not blinded to participants/parents (visible weight).

# Quotes
> "The use of a weighted blanket did not help children with ASD sleep for a longer period of time, fall asleep significantly faster, or wake less often."

# Builder takeaways
- Highest-quality evidence for any sensory tool in autism finds weighted blankets do NOT objectively help sleep — despite a USD ~$100M annual market in the US.
- Subjective preference is real, but health-claim marketing by Sensacalm/Harkla/Mosaic is unsupported by RCT evidence.
- Software wedge: sleep-routine builder + bedtime de-escalation sequencing has stronger behavioral evidence than the hardware it would replace.
- Caregivers will buy the blanket regardless. Build content around realistic expectation-setting.
