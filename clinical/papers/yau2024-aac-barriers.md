# Question
Across parents, educators, and clinicians, what are the barriers to AAC use for nonspeaking autistic individuals, and where do stakeholder views converge or diverge?

# Method
- Qualitative semi-structured interviews + focus groups.
- Reflexive thematic analysis.
- Stakeholder groups interviewed separately to allow candid responses.

# Findings
- 5 barrier themes:
  1. Stakeholder knowledge — insufficient training and AAC expertise across all groups.
  2. Stakeholder attitudes / stigma — parents reported real stigma; clinicians considered it perceived. Myth that AAC inhibits speech persists.
  3. Resources — time and money limited training and procurement.
  4. AAC user engagement — 100% of parents named user willingness as a barrier; only 27–30% of educators/clinicians did.
  5. Device fit — inadequate customization, poor inter-stakeholder communication during selection.
- States 30–50% AAC abandonment as background figure.
- Parents and clinicians attribute child non-engagement differently: parents blame child reluctance; professionals blame system fit.

# Limitations
- Western Australia sample, n=30.
- Qualitative — can identify but not quantify barrier weights.
- No child voice (nonspeaking AAC users themselves).

# Builder takeaways
- The persistent myth that AAC delays speech is a marketing problem more than a science problem — see [millar2006-aac-speech] and [schlosser2008-aac-autism-speech] for the evidence rebuttal. Address it head-on in product onboarding.
- Parent vs professional disagreement about *why* children disengage is a UX research goldmine: a product that surfaces clear engagement signals (heatmaps, touch counts, sessions) helps reconcile these views.
- Stigma is real for parents — design for discreet use in public (private mode, headphone output, neutral icon set on home screen).
