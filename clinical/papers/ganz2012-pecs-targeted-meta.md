# Question
Across PECS single-subject research, what are effects on targeted (functional communication) vs. non-targeted (speech, behavior, social) outcomes, and how do participant characteristics and PECS implementation phase moderate effects?

# Method
- Meta-analysis using Improvement Rate Difference (IRD) effect size metric.
- Single-subject PECS studies for individuals with ASD.
- Independent analysis by: targeted/non-targeted outcomes, age, learner disability profile, number of PECS phases acquired.

# Findings
- Functional communication (targeted) outcomes showed strongest effects.
- Non-targeted outcomes (behavior, social, speech) showed measurable but smaller positive effects.
- Preschool-age children showed stronger gains than older children.
- Learners with autism alone outperformed those with comorbid intellectual or multiple disabilities.
- Acquiring more PECS phases was associated with larger overall effects.

# Limitations
- Single-subject only.
- IRD has been critiqued vs. other newer non-overlap indices.
- "Phases acquired" is intermediate outcome, may correlate with intervention dosage.
- No raw IRD numbers in abstract.

# Quotes
> "Functional communication outcomes showed the strongest impact ... students advancing through more PECS protocol phases achieved superior results overall." — synthesis

# Builder takeaways
- Earlier-age intervention (preschool) is the highest-yield window for product targeting.
- Children with comorbid intellectual disability need more scaffolding and slower progression — a one-size-fits-all PECS app under-serves this segment.
- Phase progression matters — products should reward and prompt advancement through phases (don't let a user stall on Phase II for months).
