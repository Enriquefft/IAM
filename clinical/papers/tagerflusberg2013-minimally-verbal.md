# Question
What is known and unknown about minimally verbal school-aged children with autism, and what are the priorities for research and intervention?

# Method
- Narrative review and consensus statement from NIH workshop.
- Synthesis of prevalence, definitional, intervention, and assessment evidence.

# Findings
- ~30% of children with ASD remain minimally verbal even after years of intervention and educational opportunity (vs older estimate of >50%).
- No single defining set of characteristics — heterogeneous group.
- Subtypes include: no spoken language, few fixed words/phrases, primarily echolalic speech, AAC users.
- Standard cognitive and language assessments are unreliable for this population.
- Most intervention research has targeted requesting (manding) rather than broader communication.
- Evidence for AAC facilitating spoken language is limited but growing.

# Limitations
- Narrative review; no new empirical data.
- "Minimally verbal" definition varies across cited studies.
- US-centric; LATAM data absent.

# Builder takeaways
- 30% minimally-verbal figure is the canonical citation for AAC market sizing — roughly 1 in 3 autistic children needs alternative or augmentative communication support.
- Heterogeneity warning: single AAC modality (e.g., PECS, speech-generating device, sign) will not fit all 30% — product design must support multiple communication paths.
- Intervention bias toward requesting means there's a gap in social/comment/protest functions — content opportunity.
