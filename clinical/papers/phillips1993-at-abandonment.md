# Question
What proportion of assistive technology devices are abandoned, and which factors predict abandonment?

# Method
- Survey of 227 adults with various disabilities.
- Self-report on AT acquisition, use patterns, satisfaction, abandonment.
- Multiple regression to identify predictors.

# Findings
- 29.3% of all AT devices abandoned.
- Mobility aids most likely category to be abandoned.
- Peak abandonment: first year of use, then again after ~5 years.
- Four significant predictors of abandonment:
  1. Lack of user input in device selection.
  2. Easy procurement (no friction selecting "right" device).
  3. Poor device performance.
  4. Change in user needs / priorities.

# Limitations
- 1993 survey — pre-tablet, pre-modern AAC apps.
- AT broadly defined (wheelchairs, hearing aids, communication devices) — not AAC-specific.
- Self-report; recall bias likely.
- Adults only; pediatric AAC abandonment likely has different dynamics.

# Quotes
> "Technology-related policies and services need to emphasize consumer involvement and long-term needs of consumers to reduce device abandonment." — conclusion

# Builder takeaways
- The "user input in selection" finding maps directly to AAC: families that co-design symbol vocabulary, voice, and visual style abandon less. Onboarding *must* include caregiver+user customization steps as core, not optional.
- Anticipate the 1-year and 5-year abandonment cliffs with engagement campaigns / re-onboarding flows.
- "Change in needs" is inevitable — products must support graceful transitions (PECS-like → core word → text-based) inside one continuous user account, not as separate apps.
- Industry rule-of-thumb of 30%+ AT abandonment is the floor; pediatric AAC may be substantially worse (popular figure of 60% within 1 year is widely cited but lacks a single rigorous source).
