# Question
What is the prevalence of gastrointestinal (GI) symptoms in autistic children with and without co-occurring intellectual disability, and does ID affect parents' ability to report these symptoms?

# Method
- Cross-sectional analysis of clinical data 2010–2019, urban autism specialty clinic.
- Parental report on 9 GI symptoms.
- Comparison: ASD with ID (n=110) vs ASD without ID (n=198).

# Findings
- Overall: ~49% of parents reported any GI symptom (mean 0.94 of 9).
- Most common symptoms: constipation 32.3%; abdominal pain 21.2%; food refusal 19.3%.
- Across studies, GI prevalence in autism ranges 4.2%–96.8% (median 46.8%).
- Parental uncertainty was significantly higher in the ID group across subjective symptoms:
  - Abdominal pain: 25.9% (ID) vs 6.1% (no ID), OR 5.42 (95% CI 2.68–11.58).
  - Bloating: 19.4% vs 6.1%, OR 3.70 (95% CI 1.77–8.08).
  - Nausea: 15.7% vs 5.6%, OR 3.12 (95% CI 1.42–7.14).
- Excluding uncertain responses: only "other GI symptoms" differed significantly between groups (OR 3.43, 95% CI 1.36–9.10).

# Limitations
- Parent-report only; no clinical confirmation.
- Single specialty clinic — biased toward complex presentations.
- 9 symptom items only; misses functional GI subtypes.
- No autism-free comparison group.

# Builder takeaways
- Use ~50% as the working figure for "GI symptom prevalence in autistic children" — half of users will have GI considerations relevant to nutrition/wellness content.
- The ID-uncertainty finding is directly product-relevant: subjective-symptom self-report tools (pain scales, body maps) for autistic users with ID need observable proxies, not just verbal report.
- AAC products that include health/symptom vocabulary should prioritize observable GI signs (constipation, refusing food) over subjective ones (pain, nausea).
