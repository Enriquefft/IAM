# Question
What do users say to a general-purpose mental-health chatbot (Wysa) about autism, and does the chatbot meet their needs?

# Method
- Two thematic analyses of retrospective Wysa data.
- Pool 1: 1,397 messages from 908 general users mentioning autism.
- Pool 2: 277 messages from 232 self-identified autistic users.
- Emotion words extracted and categorized by valence; user-selected life challenges analyzed.

# Findings
- Top general-user themes: "others having ASD," "ASD diagnosis," "seeking help."
- Self-identified ASD users: themes of distress about diagnosis/symptoms, negative reactions from others (misunderstood, isolated, bullied), occasional positive comments.
- 80.3% of 3,725 emotion words from autistic users were negative valence.
- Most-selected life challenges (autistic users): energy/sleep (22.8%), stress/anxiety (18.1%), depression (17.3%).
- Authors: existing general-purpose chatbots are not tailored to ASD-specific needs.

# Limitations
- Self-identified diagnoses; no validation.
- Limited demographic data captured.
- No conversational context retained.
- Single chatbot (Wysa); other platforms may differ.
- Cannot infer harm or benefit from message content alone.

# Quotes
> "3,725 emotion words [were] mentioned" with 80.3% negative valence among autistic users. — Results
> Authors call for chatbots "specifically tailored to ASD populations…in collaboration with autistic individuals in app design." — Recommendations

# Builder takeaways
- Real autistic users are already using general mental-health chatbots heavily, with overwhelmingly distressed-tone interactions. Wellness/education products entering this space inherit a vulnerable user base, not a casual one.
- The most-cited life challenges are sleep, anxiety, depression — the chronic comorbidities, not autism per se. A wellness chatbot targeting only "social skills" misses the actual user demand surface.
- Calls for autistic-led co-design (echoes ASAN). Plan participatory development from the start.
