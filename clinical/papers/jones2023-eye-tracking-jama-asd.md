# Question
Can automated eye-tracking measurement of social visual engagement match expert clinical diagnosis of autism in toddlers?

# Method
- Multisite, prospective, double-blind, within-subject diagnostic accuracy study (US, 6 specialty centers).
- 475 of 499 enrolled completed (95.2%).
- Automated eye-tracking while watching social video scenes.
- Comparator: expert clinical diagnosis using ADOS-2, Mullen, DSM-5.

# Findings
- All participants: sensitivity 71.0%, specificity 80.7%.
- Certain diagnoses subgroup (n=335): sensitivity 78.0%, specificity 85.4%.
- Strong correlations: social disability r=-0.75, verbal r=0.65, nonverbal r=0.65.
- Eye-tracking results not associated with race/ethnicity (clinical dx had disparities).

# Limitations
- US specialty-center sample — not validated in primary care or LATAM.
- Reference standard itself imperfect.
- Hardware (Tobii eye tracker) and proprietary stimuli required.

# Quotes
> Test "successfully collected in 95% of enrolled children"

# Builder takeaways
- Pivotal trial behind EarliPoint FDA clearance (June 2023). Demonstrates eye-tracking biomarker is reproducible at scale.
- Hardware required: Tobii eye tracker (~USD $5,000–$30,000) + EarliPoint software subscription. Not feasible for LATAM clinical or consumer deployment.
- Webcam-based alternative (WebGazer.js) has lower fidelity — published infant validation studies show qualitative agreement but lower precision; not yet shown to reproduce ASD biomarker performance.
- Software wedge: a webcam-based screening triage that flags families for in-person assessment is the realistic LATAM angle. Cannot make diagnostic claims.
