# Question
Can an autonomous social robot deployed in the home for 30 days improve social communication in autistic children, with effects persisting outside robot interactions?

# Method
- 30-day in-home deployment of modified Jibo robot + tablet with social games.
- Triadic interaction: child + caregiver + robot, ~30 min/day.
- Outcomes: caregiver-reported responsiveness, conversation initiations, eye contact; joint attention with adults (measured outside robot context).
- Single-arm, no control group.

# Findings
- All 12 children improved on caregiver-reported social skills over 30 days.
- More responsive to communication, more conversation initiations, more eye contact.
- Joint attention improved with adults when robot not present.
- First reported autonomous (not Wizard-of-Oz) deployment of a social robot in the home for autism.

# Limitations
- n=12, no control group — single-arm case series.
- Caregiver-reported outcomes; potential expectancy bias.
- 30-day duration; no long-term follow-up published in primary paper.
- Outcomes self-selecting (engaged families recruited).
- "Autonomous" robot constrained to scripted social games.

# Quotes
> Caregivers reported improved responsiveness, more conversation initiations, and increased eye contact across all 12 participants. — Results (paraphrased from coverage)

# Builder takeaways
- Often cited as the strongest evidence for in-home autonomous social robots, but n=12 single-arm makes this evidence-level 4 — case series, not efficacy proof.
- The improvements are caregiver-reported, which is a known optimistic bias for novelty interventions.
- Demonstrates feasibility (parents will keep a robot in the home for 30 days) more than efficacy.
- For wellness products: 30-day novelty windows are real and matter — but plan for the dropoff.
