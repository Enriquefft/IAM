# Question
Is mobile VR (Floreo Joint Attention Module on Google Cardboard) feasible, safe and tolerable for joint-attention training in children with significant autism?

# Method
- Open-label single-arm pilot, no control group.
- 14 sessions over 5 weeks per participant.
- Floreo content delivered via Google Cardboard with iPad-based monitor view.
- Outcomes: monitor surveys on tolerance and enjoyment; novel pre/post play-based joint attention assessment.

# Findings
- Headset tolerated 95.4% (126/132) of session minutes.
- Apparent enjoyment 95.4% (126/132).
- 10 of 12 participants improved on at least one of: total interactions, eye contact, initiation.
- No serious adverse events; <10% minor effects (restlessness, headset discomfort).
- No dropouts due to intolerance.

# Limitations
- No control group.
- Novel assessment instrument with unestablished psychometrics.
- Very small sample.
- Communication barriers limited self-report.

# Quotes
> "95.4% (126/132) of the time participants tolerated the headset"

# Builder takeaways
- Establishes feasibility, not efficacy. Frequently cited by Floreo marketing as evidence base; the design does not support causal claims.
- Floreo's stronger evidence is the 2025 Cortica RCT (still informally reported). Both lean heavily on US delivery infrastructure (Cortica has 17 centers, none in LATAM).
- Headset tolerance was high in this severe sample — counter-intuitive datapoint for any wellness/education product avoiding VR for ASD.
