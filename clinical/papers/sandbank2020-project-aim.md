# Question
What are the comparative effects of nonpharmacological early autism interventions across intervention types and outcome domains, and how do effects change when accounting for study quality?

# Method
- Project AIM (Autism Intervention Meta-Analysis): comprehensive meta-analysis.
- 130 independent participant samples, 6,240 children (mean age 54.21 months, 84% male).
- 1,615 effect sizes synthesized.
- 7 intervention types: behavioral, developmental, NDBI, TEACCH, sensory-based, animal-assisted, technology-based.
- 15 outcome categories.
- Robust variance estimation accounting for nested effect sizes.
- Quality indicators tracked (RCT vs not, blinded outcome, caregiver vs assessor report).

# Findings
- All-studies analysis (no quality adjustment):
  - Behavioral: g = 0.24-0.46 (significant for several outcomes).
  - Developmental: g = 0.06-0.30 (social communication significant).
  - NDBI: g = -0.01-0.35 (cognition, language, play, social communication significant).
  - Sensory: g = 0.28 (NS).
  - TEACCH: g = -0.11 (NS for social communication).
  - Technology: g = 0.05-0.42 (NS).
- Quality-adjusted (RCTs only, excluding caregiver report and high detection-bias risk):
  - Only NDBI and developmental interventions retained significant effects.
  - Behavioral interventions had insufficient quality-passing data for analysis.

# Limitations
- Some intervention categories had small numbers of high-quality studies.
- Caregiver-report outcomes inflated effects relative to assessor report.
- Did not include all subtypes of every intervention (e.g., social skills training).
- Technology-based studies generally older and lower-tech than current offerings.

# Quotes
> "NDBIs and developmental interventions meet criteria as 'promising'" approaches.

> Regarding behavioral interventions: "only a fraction...were RCTs" with "majority of outcomes...from caregiver report," limiting confidence in findings.

# Builder takeaways
- Largest, highest-quality cross-intervention meta-analysis: NDBIs and developmental approaches are the only interventions with quality-adjusted significant effects.
- Behavioral (ABA/EIBI) interventions cannot be quality-adjusted because most evidence is non-RCT or caregiver-report.
- Technology-based interventions broadly under-evidenced — major gap and opportunity.
- The "tech-based effect 0.05-0.42 NS in RCTs" is the bar a wellness/education software product must clear.
