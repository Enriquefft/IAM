# Question
Can targeted joint attention or symbolic play interventions produce specific gains in those domains in young children with autism enrolled in a comprehensive ABA program?

# Method
- Single-site RCT, 58 children with autism aged 3-4 years (46 boys), already enrolled in early intervention based on ABA.
- Three arms: joint attention intervention, symbolic play intervention, control (continued ABA only).
- Intervention: 30 min/day for 5–6 weeks added to ABA program.
- Outcomes: structured joint attention assessments, mother-child interaction joint attention/play, ESCS, structured play assessment.

# Findings
- Joint attention group: significantly more showing and responsiveness to joint attention; increased child-initiated joint attention with mothers.
- Play group: more diverse symbolic play with mothers; higher play levels in formal and natural assessments.
- Specificity: each intervention targeted its specific outcome domain.

# Limitations
- Small sample, single site, short intervention duration (5-6 weeks).
- All children already in ABA program — generalizability to non-ABA contexts uncertain.
- Specific effect sizes not summarized in abstract.

# Quotes
> "Provides promising data on the specificity and generalizability of joint attention and play interventions"

# Builder takeaways
- Foundational study for JASPER framework. Demonstrates that joint attention is teachable and gains transfer to natural mother-child interaction.
- Both joint attention and symbolic play are time-and-place-bounded skills software can train (turn-taking apps, joint-attention prompts).
- Specificity finding: intervention targeted at joint attention boosts joint attention; targeted at play boosts play. Don't claim one will transfer to the other.
