# Question
Are existing core vocabulary lists used to design AAC systems for young children appropriate, given what is known about typical early-language development?

# Method
- Narrative review of 5 published core vocabulary lists for young children.
- Compared list contents against early-vocabulary developmental literature (typical first 50 words, MacArthur-Bates CDI norms).
- Examined whether word categories represented match actual early expressive vocabulary.

# Findings
- Existing core lists under-emphasize many word types that dominate early expressive vocabulary.
- Notably, common nouns (people, foods, animals) appear early in typical development but are under-represented in core lists which favor function words.
- Only 1 of 5 lists explicitly used data from children with complex communication needs.
- Authors argue core lists may not be the most appropriate single source for designing AAC vocabularies for early symbolic communicators.

# Limitations
- Narrative review, not meta-analytic.
- Subjective comparison criteria.
- Few primary studies available to review.

# Quotes
> "Core word lists may under-emphasize many of the types of words that predominate in early expressive vocabulary." — abstract

# Builder takeaways
- Don't ship pure core-vocabulary boards for the 18-month-to-3-year segment — supplement with personally meaningful nouns (family names, favorite foods, pets) from day one.
- The "core vs fringe" debate is a false binary; both belong on the screen at different proportions depending on user developmental stage.
- A product opportunity: per-user vocabulary balancing that shifts from noun-heavy at onset toward function-word-heavy as the user matures.
