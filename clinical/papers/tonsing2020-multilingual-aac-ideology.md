# Question
How do prevailing language ideologies shape AAC service-delivery practices for multilingual children and adults?

# Method
- Conceptual / theoretical paper.
- Reviews language ideology literature and applies it to AAC delivery.
- Examines policy, service models, family practices.

# Findings
- AAC literature has historically treated multilingualism as exception, not norm.
- Service-delivery default is monolingual (typically English in Anglophone countries; Spanish in Spain).
- Family preferences are often overridden by clinician/system constraints.
- Argues AAC must support all of a user's languages to be person-centered.
- Ideology — not just resource constraint — drives monolingual default.

# Builder takeaways
- For LATAM, "Spanish + English" is one config; "Spanish + Quechua" or "Spanish + Aymara" or "Portuguese + Indigenous" are real and underserved configs.
- Architect for *n* simultaneous languages from the data layer; multi-language support is not a localization afterthought.
- Family-led language selection (vs clinician-imposed) is the EBP-aligned default.
