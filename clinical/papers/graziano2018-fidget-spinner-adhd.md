# Question
Do fidget spinners help young children with ADHD with on-task attention in the classroom?

# Method
- A-B-A-B design with 60 children with ADHD.
- Fidget spinner introduced/withdrawn.
- Outcomes: observed attention.

# Findings
- Fidget spinners associated with poorer attention across treatment phases.
- Reduced some disruptive behaviors but did not improve attention.

# Effect sizes
| Outcome | Measure | Effect | 95% CI | p |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Attention | observed proportion | significantly worse with fidget | n/r | <0.05 |

# Limitations
- ADHD-only sample, not autistic-specific.
- Follow-up companion studies on cubes and bouncy bands found similarly null/negative.

# Quotes
> "fidget spinners had an overall detrimental effect on academic performance, regardless of ADHD-symptomatology"

# Builder takeaways
- Fidget hardware is largely not evidence-based; classroom use should not be recommended as intervention.
- For autism specifically: literature explicitly concludes "fidget toys were not effective" for ASD attention.
- Cheap and ubiquitous, so the wedge isn't displacing the hardware — it's replacing the marketing claim with realistic guidance.
