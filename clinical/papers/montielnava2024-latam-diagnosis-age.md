# Question
At what age do Latin American and Caribbean caregivers first notice their child's autism characteristics, when do they receive a formal diagnosis, and what factors associate with diagnostic timing?

# Method
- Cross-sectional online survey of 2,520 caregivers across six countries through Latin American Autism Spectrum Network (Red Espectro Autista Latinoamerica).
- Outcomes: median age of first concern, median age at diagnosis, median diagnostic delay.
- Regression analysis on demographic, clinical, and health-coverage predictors.

# Findings
- Median age of first parental concern: 22 months.
- Median age at autism diagnosis: 46 months.
- Median diagnostic delay (concern → diagnosis): 24 months.
- Brazil: first concern 24 months, diagnosis 60 months (~36-month delay).
- Venezuela (referenced): parents aware before age 2; diagnosis ~36 months later.
- Older age at identification, better language abilities, and public (vs private) health coverage all associated with later diagnosis.
- Other medical comorbidities and severe behavior associated with earlier diagnosis.

# Limitations
- Convenience sample via Network — over-represents engaged/connected caregivers; true population delay is likely longer.
- Online survey excludes families without internet access — strong selection bias toward urban, mid- to high-SES.
- Six-country sample omits Peru, Mexico, Colombia, Ecuador.
- Cross-sectional; no follow-up.
- All ages reported by caregiver recall — not record-verified.

# Quotes

# Builder takeaways
- 24-month median diagnostic delay across LATAM is the canonical statistic — quote it for any discussion of "diagnostic gap."
- Public-coverage delay > private-coverage delay confirms two-tier access: the public-system majority is the under-served addressable population.
- 46-month median diagnosis age means most LATAM children miss the 0–3 evidence-based early-intervention window.
- Brazil delay of 36+ months is among the worst — useful contrast point.
- Peru not in sample — inferring Peru's delay requires citing this study with explicit "estimate" qualifier.
