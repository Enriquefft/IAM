# Question
Does AAC intervention reduce, leave unchanged, or increase spoken speech production in individuals with developmental disabilities?

# Method
- Systematic review + best-evidence synthesis of literature 1975–2003.
- 23 studies identified; 6 met experimental control criteria for the rigorous subset.
- 27 cases analyzed in the best-evidence subset.
- Outcome: change in vocal speech production pre vs. during/post AAC intervention.

# Findings
- 0% of cases (0/27) showed a *decrease* in speech production.
- 11% (3/27) showed no change.
- 89% (24/27) showed gains in speech production.
- Gains were typically modest in magnitude.
- Ceiling effects in measurement may have understated true gains.

# Limitations
- 17 of 23 studies excluded for weak experimental control.
- Heterogeneous AAC modalities (sign, picture exchange, SGD) lumped.
- Mostly single-case designs.
- Published 2006; pre-iPad-era SGDs.

# Quotes
> "None of the 27 cases demonstrated decreases in speech production as a result of AAC intervention, 11% showed no change, and the majority (89%) demonstrated gains in speech." — abstract

# Builder takeaways
- This is the canonical reference for the parent-fear question "will AAC delay my child's speech?" The answer at population level is no.
- Marketing copy can defensibly state: "AAC does not slow speech; in well-controlled studies, speech improves or stays the same."
- Cite this paper directly in onboarding/FAQ when introducing symbol-based features to caregivers.
