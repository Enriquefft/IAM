# Question
Does high-tech AAC meet evidence-based-practice criteria (per What Works Clearinghouse) for teaching social-communication skills to people with ASD or ID?

# Method
- Systematic review of 18 multiple-baseline / multiple-probe single-case designs (across 17 articles).
- Quality assessed against WWC Single-Case Design Standards.
- Extended methodological standards: participant description, settings, baseline integrity, maintenance, generalization, procedural integrity, social validity.
- Comparison sub-analysis: high-tech vs low-tech AAC outcomes.

# Findings
- High-tech AAC meets EBP criteria for teaching social-communication to ASD/ID populations.
- Most studies measured manding/requesting; fewer addressed comments, joint attention, or conversational repair.
- Comparison studies did *not* show high-tech significantly superior to low-tech AAC.
- Maintenance and generalization data were inconsistently reported.
- Procedural-integrity reporting was a major gap.

# Limitations
- Single-case designs only.
- Heterogeneous outcomes; many studies measure manding only.
- Variable procedural-integrity documentation.
- "High-tech" definition spans dedicated SGDs and tablet apps interchangeably.

# Quotes
> "Using high-tech AAC to teach social-communication skills to individuals with autism spectrum disorder or intellectual disabilities and complex communication needs can be considered an evidence-based practice." — abstract

# Builder takeaways
- High-tech AAC is now an evidence-based practice — table stakes for procurement, IEPs, and clinician acceptance.
- The "high-tech is better" narrative is not supported by head-to-head data — product positioning should rest on *accessibility, affordability, customization*, not pure efficacy claims over PECS cards.
- Maintenance/generalization is the under-served gap. A product that proves maintenance at 6 and 12 months will be the differentiator.
