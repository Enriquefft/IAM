# Question
How can applied behavior analysis (ABA) be reformed to address legitimate criticisms from autistic community while remaining evidence-based?

# Method
- Conceptual/position paper from BCBA authors and field leaders.
- Synthesizes neurodiversity literature, autistic adult testimony, and behavior-analytic literature.
- No primary data.

# Findings
- Identifies specific harms historically associated with ABA: masking/identity erasure, compliance over assent, escape extinction with high-hours protocols, exclusive focus on the four behavioral functions.
- Proposes reform agenda: assent-based protocols, reorientation of goals from "indistinguishability" to client-defined quality of life, reassessment of stereotypy reduction targets, autistic co-investigators on research, trauma-informed care.
- Argues that critiques cannot be dismissed as anti-science; field must respond.

# Limitations
- No empirical data; reform proposals untested.
- Authors acknowledge no consensus on reform within ABA professional bodies.

# Quotes
> "The concerns raised by the autistic community cannot, morally or ethically, be swept aside and devalued due to the speaker's identification as autistic."

> "The neurodiversity perspective contends that living in a society designed for non-autistic people contributes to, and exacerbates, many of the daily living challenges that autistic people experience."

# Builder takeaways
- Established ABA practitioners themselves acknowledge the critique — product positioning should not mock or dismiss neurodiversity framing.
- Assent-based, child-led design (capturing dissent signals; allowing escape from non-preferred tasks) aligns with where ABA is moving and where neurodiversity advocates are.
- Goals framed as "client-defined quality of life" survive both ABA-evidence and neurodiversity scrutiny.
