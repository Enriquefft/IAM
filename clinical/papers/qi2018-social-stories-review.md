# Question
Are Social Stories an evidence-based practice for individuals with autism spectrum disorder per What Works Clearinghouse (WWC) standards?

# Method
- Systematic review using WWC SCRD standards.
- 22 single-case research design studies.
- 4 nonoverlap indices computed.
- Visual analysis vs nonoverlap indices comparison.

# Findings
- All 22 studies met WWC design standards with or without reservations.
- 5% (1/22) provided strong evidence of causal relation; 27% (6/22) moderate; 68% (15/22) weak.
- Verdict by visual analysis: NOT evidence-based practice.
- Verdict by nonoverlap indices: WOULD qualify as EBP.
- Methodological discrepancy between two analytical approaches yields conflicting conclusions.

# Effect sizes
| Index type | Direction | Notes |

# Limitations
- Single-case designs only — no group RCTs.
- Methodological discrepancy unresolved by review.
- Most studies did not follow Carol Gray's 10 defining criteria for proper Social Stories construction.

# Quotes
> "Social stories intervention would not be considered an evidence-based practice (EBP) for individuals with ASD based on visual analysis, but would be deemed an EBP based on nonoverlap indices."

# Builder takeaways
- Social Stories sit in evidentiary limbo — the strongest claim defensible is "promising but contested."
- Most "Social Stories" in classroom use don't meet Gray's criteria — fidelity is the gap.
- Software opportunity: AI-generated Social Stories that automatically conform to Gray's 10 criteria — fixes the fidelity problem at scale.
