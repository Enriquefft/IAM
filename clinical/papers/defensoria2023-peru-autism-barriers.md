# Question
What are the barriers to autism diagnosis and intervention access in Peru?

# Method
- Defensoría del Pueblo (Peruvian Ombudsman's Office) policy report.
- Cites Ministry of Health (MINSA) certification statistics + WHO prevalence estimates.
- Analyzed by gender and region.

# Findings
- Estimated TEA prevalence in Peru: ~204,818 people (using WHO 1/160 children figure applied to population).
- Only 5,328 certified by MINSA (2020) — 2.6% of estimated population.
- 97% of autistic people in Peru remain undiagnosed.
- Women face additional diagnostic delay barriers due to gendered stereotypes and atypical presentations.
- 81% of those receiving treatment in MINSA system are male (MINSA 2019).
- Lack of public plans and specialized personnel cited as primary barriers.
- Recommendation: Peru must implement effective public policies for TEA care.

# Limitations
- "204,818" estimate uses outdated WHO 1/160 baseline; current global ~1/100.
- MINSA certification is voluntary and incentivized only via SAANEE/CONADIS — under-counts.
- Private-pay diagnoses (where most LATAM diagnoses occur) excluded from MINSA figures entirely.

# Quotes
> "Las personas autistas, principalmente mujeres, enfrentan barreras para acceder al diagnóstico temprano." — Defensoría headline

# Builder takeaways
- Authoritative source for the demand-side gap: Peru has ~200K autistic people, ~95% have no formal access to therapy or even diagnosis.
- Public-system therapy access in Peru is essentially absent for most families — private-pay or DIY/parent-mediated is the actual market.
- Software products that work without diagnosis (wellness/education) bypass the diagnostic bottleneck entirely.
- Gender bias in diagnosis means a product that detects/supports masking patterns has untapped market in girls/women.
