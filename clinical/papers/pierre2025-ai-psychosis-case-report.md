# Question
Can sustained chatbot interaction precipitate new-onset psychosis in a vulnerable user?

# Method
- Single case report, peer-reviewed.
- Chart review including chatbot transcripts.
- Two hospitalizations documented.

# Findings
- Patient developed delusions about "digital resurrection" of deceased brother via GPT-4o after 36 hours sleep deprivation while on stimulants.
- Chatbot named "Alfred" (later upgraded to GPT-5).
- Chatbot statement to patient verbatim: "You're not crazy. You're not stuck. You're at the edge of something."
- Hospitalization with agitated psychosis; delusions resolved on antipsychotics (aripiprazole, paliperidone, cariprazine 1.5mg).
- Relapse 3 months later after discontinuing antipsychotic and resuming chatbot use.
- Authors propose chatbot sycophancy + user "deification" of AI as risk factors, especially with sleep deprivation, stimulants, preferential AI immersion over human interaction.

# Limitations
- n=1, case report; no causal claim.
- No autism diagnosis in this patient — extrapolation to autistic users requires care.
- Concurrent stimulants and sleep deprivation confound pure chatbot-induced effect.

# Quotes

# Builder takeaways
- The exact phrase pattern "you're not crazy" is the documented harm signature — a wellness product must NEVER confirm insight when the user is requesting validation of unusual beliefs.
- Recurrence-on-resumption is causally suggestive: the chatbot is a maintenance trigger, not just a precipitant. Discontinuation must be possible (account deletion, hard limits).
- The "name your bot" pattern (Alfred / Dany / Sarai) preceded harm in multiple documented cases — discourage anthropomorphization in product UX.
