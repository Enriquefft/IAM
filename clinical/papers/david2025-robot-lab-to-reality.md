# Question
Do robot-assisted therapy interventions for young children with ASD achieve equivalent outcomes to conventional treatment in both controlled clinical (efficacy) and real-world (effectiveness) settings?

# Method
- Two parallel RCTs.
- Efficacy trial: 69 children (mean 4.4 yrs); 12 biweekly in-clinic robot-assisted therapy sessions.
- Effectiveness trial: 63 children (mean 5.9 yrs); 5 sessions in simplified settings (schools/homes).
- Targets: joint attention, imitation, turn-taking.
- Comparator: conventional treatment.

# Findings
- Efficacy trial: clinical robot therapy achieved equivalent outcomes to conventional treatment, with significantly increased patient engagement.
- Effectiveness trial: equivalent outcomes despite lower dosage in community settings.
- Authors frame as evidence that portable, cost-effective setups can produce comparable benefits.

# Limitations
- Two trials, total n=132 — meaningful but not large.
- Equivalence (non-superiority) is the primary frame — robot is not better, just not worse.
- Authors include robot-platform stakeholders (likely conflict).
- Behind paywall; full effect-size CI extraction limited.
- Engagement is a process measure, not a clinical outcome.

# Quotes
> Clinical robot therapy "achieve[d] equivalent outcomes to conventional treatment but with a significant increase in the patients' engagement." — Results

# Builder takeaways
- The most-cited 2025 paper for "robots work in autism" is actually a non-inferiority study — robot ≠ better than human therapy, just equally engaging.
- "Engagement" is the only differentiator. For a wellness/education product, engagement IS valuable — but it's not a clinical outcome.
- The effectiveness arm (5 sessions, simpler setting) showing equivalence is the more relevant data point for low-resource deployment in LATAM/Peru.
