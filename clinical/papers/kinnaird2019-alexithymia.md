# Question
What is the prevalence of alexithymia (difficulty identifying and describing one's own emotions) in autism vs neurotypical populations?

# Method
- Systematic review and meta-analysis of studies using the Toronto Alexithymia Scale (TAS-20 or TAS-26).
- 15 studies meeting criteria.

# Findings
- Weighted mean prevalence of alexithymia: 49.9% in ASD vs 4.9% in neurotypical.
- Range across ASD studies: 33.3%–63%.
- Range across neurotypical studies: 0%–20%.
- Risk ratio for alexithymia in ASD vs neurotypical: 6.50 (95% CI 3.26–12.93).
- Authors emphasize alexithymia is "common, rather than universal" in autism — supports view that emotional difficulties stem from co-occurring alexithymia rather than autism per se.

# Limitations
- All studies used self-report TAS — questionable validity in populations with the construct being measured.
- Sample dominated by adolescents/adults; less data on children.
- Selection bias toward higher-functioning autistic adults able to complete TAS.

# Builder takeaways
- Half of autistic users have difficulty identifying/describing their own emotional state — emotion-labeling features in wellness products must offer scaffolds (visual scales, body-map prompts, externalized cues) rather than expecting verbal self-report.
- Emotion-tracking is not a niche concern; it intersects with the most common autism-adjacent need (after sensory regulation).
- Co-occurring alexithymia is a candidate subgroup for separate product profiles.
