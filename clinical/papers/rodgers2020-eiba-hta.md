# Question
Are early intensive applied behaviour analysis (EIBI) interventions clinically effective and cost-effective for autistic children, and what is the strength of the evidence base?

# Method
- Systematic review and cost-effectiveness analysis commissioned by UK NIHR.
- 20 included studies (33 publications, 1987–2017): 3 RCTs, remainder observational/non-randomised.
- Children 24–66 months (median 37.4 months).
- Primary outcomes: cognitive ability, adaptive behaviour, language, autism symptom severity.
- Risk-of-bias: Cochrane RoB tool (RCTs); ROBINS-I (non-RCTs).

# Findings
- Of 20 studies, only 3 were RCTs; remainder rated "serious" or "high" risk of bias on at least one ROBINS-I domain.
- Confounding pervasive in non-RCTs (treatment allocation by parental preference; SES differences between groups).
- True blinded outcome assessment infeasible across nearly all studies.
- Pooled estimates downgraded due to bias risk; authors warn observed effects likely overestimate true effect.
- Cost-effectiveness conclusion: insufficient evidence to recommend widespread EIBI commissioning at current evidence level.

# Limitations
- Authors' own caveat: limited high-quality RCT evidence base after 30 years of EIBI research.
- Heterogeneous interventions all labelled "EIBI" — UCLA-style Lovaas vs ESDM lumped.
- Most studies pre-dated current naturalistic-behavioural standards.

# Quotes
> "All non-randomised studies were rated as being at 'serious' or 'high' risk of bias for at least one outcome on one domain."

# Builder takeaways
- Definitive UK gov assessment: EIBI evidence base is weaker than its commercial footprint suggests.
- A wellness/education product should not anchor claims to EIBI-style "comprehensive" outcomes; this report would be cited against you.
- Reinforces case for narrowly-scoped, parent-implemented, skill-specific software vs end-to-end therapy claims.
