# Question
What are the updated, quality-adjusted effect estimates for nonpharmacological early autism interventions across intervention type and outcome domain?

# Method
- Updated Project AIM systematic review and meta-analysis published in BMJ.
- 252 studies, 13,304 participants under age 8.
- Multiple intervention types: behavioral, developmental, NDBI, sensory, TEACCH, animal-assisted, technology-based.
- Robust variance estimation with small-sample bias correction.
- Quality-adjusted analysis: RCT-only, excluding caregiver report and high-detection-bias studies.

# Findings
- Unadjusted (all studies):
  - Behavioral: improved social-emotional / challenging behavior g = 0.58; NS for social communication.
  - Developmental: improved social communication g = 0.28.
  - NDBI: improved adaptive behavior g = 0.23, autism characteristics g = 0.38, language g = 0.16, play g = 0.19, social communication g = 0.35.
- Quality-adjusted (RCTs only):
  - Only developmental and NDBI retained significant summary effects.
  - When restricted further to no caregiver report and low detection-bias risk: only NDBI effect on autism diagnostic characteristics remained significant.
- Adverse events: "poorly monitored, but possibly common."

# Limitations
- Adverse events under-reported across all intervention types.
- Quality filtering shrinks effective sample dramatically.
- Heterogeneity across "behavioral" category (Lovaas-style EIBI vs ABA-light).

# Quotes
> "Adverse events were poorly monitored, but possibly common."

> When restricted to RCTs excluding caregiver report and high detection bias: only NDBI effects on autism diagnostic characteristics retained significance.

# Builder takeaways
- Most authoritative cross-intervention meta-analysis as of 2026 — published in BMJ, the highest-impact medical journal.
- NDBI is the only intervention class with quality-adjusted effects across multiple outcomes.
- "Adverse events possibly common" is a critical builder warning: any product based on an intervention should track adverse events, dropouts, and parent/child distress.
- Behavioral interventions show effects on challenging behavior (g=0.58) — one defensible behavioral framing for a product.
