# Question
In community classrooms, do speech-generating-device (free open-source iPad app) interventions outperform PECS for teaching social-communication behaviors to children with autism?

# Method
- Pilot community-based RCT.
- 35 school-age children randomized to PECS or SGD.
- 4-month classroom-based intervention.
- SGD condition used a free open-source iPad app implementing the same PECS teaching sequence.
- Outcomes: functional communication acts (PECS-protocol-aligned).

# Findings
- Both arms produced significant improvements in functional communication.
- No significant difference between PECS and SGD arms.
- Both modalities feasible in community classroom delivery.

# Effect sizes

| Functional communication | between-arm | no significant difference | n/a | ns |

# Limitations
- Pilot study, n=35; underpowered for non-inferiority claim.
- Single-region (Ireland) community sample.
- 4-month duration; no long-term follow-up reported.
- SGD was a specific free app, not a representative comparison of all SGDs.

# Quotes
> "Both high-tech and low-tech AAC approaches resulted in significant improvements in communication, and that these improvements did not differ" between the groups. — summary

# Builder takeaways
- A *free* open-source SGD performed as well as PECS in classroom delivery — strong precedent that low-cost AAC is clinically viable.
- For a wellness/education product targeting LATAM where dedicated SGDs are unaffordable, this paper underwrites the "tablet-app is enough" claim.
- Community classroom feasibility was demonstrated; the deployment context aligns with school distribution channels.
