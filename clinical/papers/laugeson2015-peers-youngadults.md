# Question
Does the UCLA PEERS for Young Adults caregiver-assisted social skills program improve social skills and reduce social impairment in young adults with ASD?

# Method
- Single-site RCT, n=22 (12 treatment, 10 delayed control).
- Ages 18-24, high-functioning ASD.
- 16 weekly 90-minute sessions.
- Caregiver-assisted (parent or other caregiver).
- Outcomes: social skills (SSRS), social engagement frequency, social skills knowledge, autism symptoms (SRS).
- 16-week follow-up.

# Findings
- Significant improvements in overall social skills, frequency of social engagement, social skills knowledge.
- Reduced autism symptoms (SRS social responsiveness).
- Most gains persisted at 16-week follow-up; some new improvements emerged.

# Effect sizes
| Outcome | Measure | Effect | 95% CI | p |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Social skills | SSRS | sig pre-post | n/r | <.05 |
| Social engagement frequency | Self-report log | sig pre-post | n/r | <.05 |
| Knowledge of social skills | TASSK | sig pre-post | n/r | <.05 |
| Autism symptoms | SRS | sig pre-post | n/r | <.05 |

# Limitations
- Tiny sample (n=22).
- High-functioning only; generalization to higher-support-need adults uncertain.
- Caregiver requirement may exclude independent autistic adults.
- No long-term follow-up beyond 16 weeks.

# Quotes
> "Most treatment gains persisted at 16-week follow-up with additional new improvements noted."

# Builder takeaways
- Strongest published evidence for adult autism social skills training.
- 16-week curriculum is well-defined and software-augmentable (homework tracking, role-play scripts, get-together planner).
- Caregiver-assistance requirement creates two distinct user roles — design implications.
