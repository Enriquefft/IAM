# Question
Does Relationship Development Intervention (RDI) produce improvements in autism diagnosis, flexibility, and educational placement?

# Method
- Retrospective uncontrolled follow-up.
- 16 children received RDI between 2000-2005.
- Outcomes: ADOS, ADI-R, flexibility ratings, school placement at minimum 30-month follow-up.
- Single clinic, single intervention developer (Gutstein) as PI.

# Findings
- All 16 met autism diagnostic criteria pre-treatment; none met criteria at follow-up (per authors).
- Improvements in ADOS-rated flexibility.
- More children moved to less restrictive school placements.

# Effect sizes
| Outcome | Measure | Effect | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| ADOS diagnostic status | ADOS | 16/16 → 0/16 met criteria | uncontrolled |
| Flexibility | ADOS scoring | improved | uncontrolled |

# Limitations
- No control or comparison group.
- Parent self-selection.
- Single clinic; intervention developer is study author (severe COI).
- Retrospective, no blinded assessment.
- ADOS scoring repeated at follow-up by same/related raters — practice effects + bias.
- No replication by independent groups (as of 2026).

# Quotes
> Limitations acknowledged by authors: "lack of a control or comparison group, constraints on age and IQ of treated children, parent self-selection, and parent education conducted through a single clinic setting."

# Builder takeaways
- The RDI evidence base is essentially this single retrospective study by the developer, with no independent replication.
- Despite extreme dissemination commercially, RDI is NOT an empirically supported intervention by NSP/NPDC standards.
- Should not be a target intervention for product positioning; can be acknowledged as a parental-acceptability/relationship-focused option.
