# Question
What are the prevalence, classification, and clinical correlates of atypical sensory characteristics in autism spectrum disorder?

# Method
- Book-chapter review.
- Synthesizes published prevalence, neurophysiology, and behavioral data.
- Uses Dunn's Four Quadrant Model (neurological thresholds × behavioral response strategies).

# Findings
- Atypical sensory characteristics in ~90% of children with ASD.
- Auditory hypersensitivity reported range: 15–100% (wide due to measurement heterogeneity).
- Sensory processing disorder in general population: ≥5%.
- Three primary modalities described:
  - Auditory: hypersensitivity, filtering deficits, abnormal startle responses.
  - Visual: hypersensitivity to flashing lights, enhanced peripheral responses, spatial-frequency dissociations (hyper at high SF, hypo at low SF).
  - Tactile: hyper- and hypo-responsiveness, atypical affective touch processing.
- Sensory issues correlate with anxiety, attention problems, self-injury, behavioral problems, sleep disturbance, and GI symptoms.

# Limitations
- Review chapter; not a primary meta-analysis.
- Olfactory, gustatory, vestibular, proprioceptive, interoception not given separate prevalence.
- Wide auditory range (15–100%) limits utility of the headline figure.

# Quotes
> Sensory characteristics affect "approximately 90%" of children with ASD. — Chapter overview
> "Sensory problems in ASD have been reported to be associated with many of the core symptoms of ASD and other symptoms such as anxiety, attention problems, self-injury, behavioral problems, sleep disturbances, and gastrointestinal symptoms." — Chapter conclusion

# Builder takeaways
- Plan for ~9 in 10 autistic users having atypical sensory profiles — sensory accommodations are a default, not a feature.
- Auditory and visual hypersensitivity are the most commonly affected modalities — design choices around audio (loudness ceilings, no sudden sounds) and visuals (no high-frequency flicker, calm color palettes) carry largest population impact.
- Sensory profile correlates with sleep, anxiety, behavior — addressing sensory regulation has compound benefit across domains.
