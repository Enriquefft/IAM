# Summary
Adapted Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) for anxiety in cognitively able autistic youth has the **strongest mental-health intervention evidence in the autism literature**. The 2021 meta-analysis ([sharma2021-cbt-anxiety-meta], 19 RCTs, n=833) reports clinician-rated anxiety g=**0.88 (large)**, parent-rated g=0.40, child self-rated g=0.25 — a **3-fold informant discrepancy** that itself becomes a methodological caution. Manualized adapted protocols (BIACA — Behavioral Interventions for Anxiety in Children with Autism; Coping Cat; Cool Kids; Exploring Feelings) have replicated effects across multiple labs. Evidence in **depression and other comorbidities** is much thinner. The dominant evidence gap: **autistic youth with IQ < 70 are largely excluded from existing CBT trials**. Software opportunity is substantial — manualized CBT is structured, session-based, homework-heavy, and well-suited to digital delivery; the key adaptations are visual schedules, concrete language, special-interest-driven examples, and emotion-recognition scaffolds.

# What it is
- **CBT** = Cognitive Behavioral Therapy: structured, time-limited psychotherapy targeting maladaptive thoughts and behaviors via cognitive restructuring + behavioral exposure + skills training.
- **Adapted CBT for ASD**: CBT modified for autistic learners. Common adaptations:
  - Visual supports (thought records, emotion thermometers, situation diagrams).
  - Concrete, literal language; reduced metaphor.
  - Special-interest integration (using the child's interest as content for examples).
  - Predictable session structure with visual agenda.
  - Emotion-recognition scaffolds (face cards, emotion vocabulary).
  - Parent involvement in skill generalization and exposure homework.
  - Slower pacing, more repetition, more concrete practice of cognitive techniques.

# Origin
- CBT generally: Aaron Beck, Albert Ellis (1960s–1970s).
- Adaptation for autism: pioneered by Susan Williams White, Eric Storch, Connor Kerns, Tony Attwood, others (2000s onward).
- BIACA — Wood & Drahota lab, UCLA. First manualized adapted CBT for ASD-specific anxiety; multiple RCTs.

# Target population
- Cognitively able autistic children, adolescents, young adults (IQ > 70 typical).
- Most evidence in anxiety disorders (specific phobia, separation, social, generalized, OCD).
- Emerging evidence in depression, anger management, sleep.
- Autistic adults: smaller evidence base; growing in 2020s.

# Delivery model + intensity
- Manualized: 10–16 weekly sessions of 60–90 min typical.
- Therapist-delivered (psychologist, psychiatrist, trained clinician) + parent involvement.
- Homework: between-session exposure tasks, thought records, behavioral experiments.
- Group, individual, or hybrid formats — all show evidence.
- Telehealth delivery: feasible and increasingly evidence-supported (general CBT literature; autism-specific telehealth CBT trials emerging).

# Evidence summary

## Headline effects
- [sharma2021-cbt-anxiety-meta] (2021 BMC Psychology, 19 RCTs, n=833):
  - Clinician-rated anxiety g = **0.88** (95% CI 0.55–1.12), large effect, k=11.
  - Parent-rated anxiety g = **0.40** (0.24–0.56), small-medium, k=18.
  - Child self-rated anxiety g = **0.25** (0.06–0.43), small, k=13.
  - Risk of bias: 63% rated "some concerns"; only 37% pre-registered.
  - Follow-up data sparse.
- [hume2021-ebp-third-gen]: Cognitive behavioral / instructional strategies confirmed as EBP at component level.

## Direction
- **Anxiety**: reliable significant effects across multiple RCTs. Largest effects in clinician-rated outcomes; informant discrepancy is the dominant methodological caution.
- **Depression**: minimal autism-specific RCT evidence as of 2026; general CBT-for-depression literature does not directly translate.
- **OCD in ASD**: emerging evidence (some BIACA trials include OCD; specific ASD+OCD adaptations underway).
- **Sleep, anger, emotion regulation**: emerging literature; smaller trials.

# Controversies

## Informant discrepancy
- Clinician-rated effects (g=0.88) are 3.5× child self-rated effects (g=0.25). Multiple explanations possible:
  - Clinician outcomes may capture functional anxiety reduction better than child self-report (children may lack interoceptive insight to report internal change).
  - Alexithymia ([kinnaird2019-alexithymia]) — common in autism — limits emotional self-report accuracy.
  - Demand characteristics may inflate clinician outcomes.
- The discrepancy is itself an evidence-quality flag.

## IQ exclusion
- Most CBT-for-ASD trials require IQ > 70 (some > 80). Autistic individuals with co-occurring intellectual disability are largely excluded from this evidence base.
- Adaptation work for lower-IQ populations is preliminary.

## Diagnostic complexity
- ASD + anxiety overlap symptomatically (avoidance, repetitive behavior, social withdrawal). Distinguishing anxiety from autism-itself is clinically nontrivial and may inflate baseline anxiety scores in some cohorts.

## Cultural validity
- Most evidence is Anglo-American, middle-class, English-language.
- Spanish-language and LATAM cultural-adaptation studies are limited.
- Cool Kids has Australian and international adaptations; some Spanish-language work.

# Cost
- **US**: USD $80–$200/session for psychologist-delivered CBT. Insurance varies. Total programme cost USD $800–$3,000.
- **EU**: NHS-delivered in UK (limited); private similar to US.
- **LATAM**: USD $30–$80/session in private psychology practice. Total programme cost USD $300–$1,500. Specialized ASD+anxiety expertise rare.
- **Peru**: USD $40–$100/session in Lima private practice. Few clinicians with formal adapted-CBT-for-ASD training. CETNA and university-affiliated clinics have some capacity.

# Availability in Peru / LATAM
- General CBT availability is relatively good in capital cities (clinical psychology profession well-established in MX, AR, BR, CL).
- **ASD-adapted CBT specifically** is much scarcer — requires clinician with both CBT and autism expertise.
- Spanish-language adapted CBT manuals: Cool Kids has Spanish version; BIACA Spanish-language work in progress; Coping Cat Spanish translations exist.
- Public-system mental-health coverage for autistic individuals is severely limited across LATAM. See [lai2019-mental-health-comorbidity], [defensoria2023-peru-autism-barriers].

# Tech opportunities

## High-leverage software wedges

- **Adapted CBT modules for anxiety in autistic youth**: structured, session-based, manualized — directly translatable to digital delivery. Integrate visual supports, concrete language, special-interest customization. Spanish-first content for LATAM.
- **Emotion thermometer / mood tracking**: common CBT primitive; enhanced for autism with visual scales, photo-based emotion recognition, alexithymia-aware language.
- **Thought record + cognitive restructuring tools**: structured forms with visual scaffolding (ABC: Activating event → Belief → Consequence; alternative thought generation prompts).
- **Exposure hierarchy planner**: visual ladder of feared situations, ordered by anxiety SUDS rating; in-app exposure tracking.
- **Special-interest integration engine**: AI-personalized CBT examples using the user's stated special interests (trains, dinosaurs, Pokémon, Roblox, etc.) — concrete and motivating.
- **Therapist + client hybrid platform**: CBT homework between sessions delivered/tracked in app; clinician dashboard for progress monitoring and homework review. Mirrors PMI coach-in-the-loop pattern.
- **Parent involvement dashboard**: between-session exposure-coaching prompts for parents, exposure-completion tracking.
- **Multi-informant outcome tracking**: PROMIS / SCARED / RCADS / parent + child versions side-by-side, surfacing the informant-discrepancy pattern as a clinical insight rather than hiding it.

## Honest scope

- **Anxiety is the empirical anchor**. Depression, anger, sleep — emerging literature; build with explicit evidence-tier labeling.
- **Pure-AI-therapist for autism without human clinician oversight has documented risks** — see [iftikhar2025-llm-mental-health-ethics], [auyeung2025-psychogenic-machine], [pierre2025-ai-psychosis-case-report], [aghakhani2023-wysa-asd-messages]. The empirically supported pattern is software + human clinician, not software alone.
- **AI chatbots for autism mental health**: extremely cautious territory. Documented incidents in adjacent populations ([incident-setzer-character-ai-2024], [incident-character-ai-jf-texas-2024], [incident-chail-replika-windsor-2021]).
- **Adapted CBT delivery beyond Spanish-language constraints**: bilingual delivery (Spanish + English), simplified-language modes, communication-needs-aware adaptations.

# Evidence
- [sharma2021-cbt-anxiety-meta] — 19-RCT meta-analysis; clinician g=0.88, parent g=0.40, child g=0.25.
- [hume2021-ebp-third-gen] — Cognitive-behavioral / instructional strategies confirmed EBP at component level.
- [kinnaird2019-alexithymia] — Alexithymia in autism — relevant to self-report limitation.
- [lai2019-mental-health-comorbidity] — Mental-health comorbidity in autism; population-level need.

# Open questions
- Adapted CBT for autistic youth with co-occurring intellectual disability — minimal data.
- Adapted CBT for **depression** in autism — emerging trials, no anchoring meta-analysis as of 2026.
- Long-term outcomes (1+ year follow-up) — sparse data.
- Telehealth delivery RCTs in autism specifically — growing but limited.
- LATAM cultural-adaptation efficacy studies — essentially absent.
- Self-directed app-only delivery vs coach-augmented delivery — efficacy comparison in autistic populations.
- Adapted CBT for autistic adults — smaller evidence base than youth.

# Builder takeaways
- **Anxiety is the empirical anchor for an autism + mental-health software product.** Largest effect sizes in the autism intervention literature for any non-skill-acquisition outcome.
- **Manualized adapted CBT is unusually well-suited to digital delivery**: structured sessions, defined homework, written/visual content. Existing manuals (BIACA, Cool Kids, Exploring Feelings) provide the curriculum scaffolding.
- **Spanish-first, LATAM-cultural-fit content** is a content-only wedge with high leverage and limited competition.
- **Multi-informant outcome tracking** addresses the documented informant discrepancy honestly. A product that surfaces parent + child + clinician ratings side-by-side is more methodologically honest than single-source reporting.
- **Special-interest customization** is a high-impact differentiator — standard CBT examples (work stress, social rejection in neurotypical contexts) often fail to engage autistic users; AI-customized examples leveraging the user's stated interests are technically feasible and pedagogically aligned.
- **Coach-in-the-loop is the empirically supported pattern** — pure-AI-therapist for autism is high-liability territory. Therapist-augmenting tools (between-session homework, exposure tracking) are the safer wedge.
- **IQ and language profile as eligibility filter**: the current evidence base is predominantly IQ > 70. A product should be honest about target population and not over-claim for higher-support-needs autistic users.
- **Avoid AI-chatbot-as-therapist framing entirely**. Documented incidents in adjacent populations + absence of autism-specific safety evidence + alexithymia + self-report limitations all argue against this design pattern.
- **Public-system mental-health coverage gap in LATAM** is a real population-level need ([lai2019-mental-health-comorbidity], [defensoria2023-peru-autism-barriers]) that creates demand for low-cost private alternatives — the wellness/education positioning is structurally compatible.
- **Adolescents and young adults**: this age band has high anxiety prevalence in autism, the strongest CBT evidence, and is underserved by autism software (most products target young children). Strategic positioning option.
