# Summary
Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) — and its high-intensity variant Early Intensive Behavioral Intervention (EIBI / Lovaas-style) — is the most-funded and most-disputed autism intervention. The Cochrane review ([reichow2018-eibi-cochrane], 5 studies, n=219) reports IQ MD=15.4 and adaptive behavior MD=9.6 favoring EIBI, but rates all evidence GRADE **low** and notes "weak evidence." A 14-RCT meta-analysis ([yu2020-aba-meta], n=555) found **only expressive language** statistically significant; autism symptoms, IQ, adaptive behavior, daily living, and repetitive behaviors were all NS. Project AIM 2023 ([sandbank2023-project-aim-update]) cannot quality-adjust the behavioral category because most evidence is non-RCT or caregiver-report, and adverse events are "poorly monitored, but possibly common." The UK NIHR HTA ([rodgers2020-eiba-hta], 20 studies, 1500 children) reaches the same low-confidence directional conclusion. The neurodiversity / autistic self-advocate critique ([kupferstein2018-aba-ptsd], [mathur2024-aba-neurodiversity], ASAN) argues ABA causes lasting harm via compliance training and masking. The current honest position: ABA is **evidence-supported for discrete skill acquisition (especially expressive language) at low GRADE**, the marketing claim that ABA "treats autism" is **not supported**, and the ethical critique deserves serious engagement rather than dismissal.

# What it is
- **ABA** = Applied Behavior Analysis: a discipline applying operant-conditioning principles (antecedent–behavior–consequence, reinforcement schedules) to socially significant behavior change. Originated with Skinner; applied to autism by Lovaas (UCLA, 1960s–1980s).
- **DTT** = Discrete Trial Training: a teaching format within ABA — short repeated trials of stimulus → response → reinforcement, typically table-based, 1:1.
- **EIBI** = Early Intensive Behavioral Intervention: 20–40 hr/week of (often DTT-heavy) ABA delivered to children under 5, originally targeting Lovaas's claim of "recovery" in 47% of children (1987). The 1987 study has not been replicated to that magnitude.
- **NDBI** evolution: Modern ABA has shifted toward naturalistic, child-led delivery — ESDM, PRT, JASPER all incorporate ABA principles within an NDBI frame. See [ndbi].

# Origin and target population
- Lovaas (UCLA, 1987) published the first major efficacy claim — n=19 EIBI, n=40 control. The "47% recovery" finding has been heavily critiqued for diagnostic instability and selection bias.
- Original target: children 2–5 with autism diagnoses, intervention 40 hr/week × 2+ years.
- Current US insurance mandates (~all 50 states) cover ABA for autism under varying age/dose limits; this has driven enormous private-equity-backed ABA expansion in the US (>$10B market).

# Delivery model and intensity
- BCBA (Board Certified Behavior Analyst) supervises program; RBTs (Registered Behavior Technicians) deliver direct hours.
- Standard EIBI dose: 25–40 hr/week × 1–3 years. Lower-dose ABA: 10–20 hr/week.
- Typical session: combination of DTT, natural-environment teaching, parent training.

# Evidence summary

## Headline effect sizes
- [reichow2018-eibi-cochrane] (1 RCT + 4 CCTs, n=219): IQ MD=15.4 (95% CI 9.3–21.6), adaptive behavior MD=9.6 (5.6–13.6), expressive language SMD=0.51 (0.12–0.90), receptive language SMD=0.55 (0.23–0.87). Autism severity and problem behavior NS. **All GRADE low**.
- [yu2020-aba-meta] (14 RCTs, n=555): expressive language d=−3.52 (−6.31 to −0.72), p=0.01 (large but I²=94%). All other outcomes NS or trend.
- [rodgers2020-eiba-hta] (NIHR, 20 studies): cost-effectiveness uncertain; effect estimates consistent with Cochrane direction but evidence "limited and of poor quality."
- [sandbank2020-project-aim] / [sandbank2023-project-aim-update]: Behavioral interventions show g=0.24–0.46 in unadjusted analysis; **insufficient quality-passing data to retain significance after RCT/blind-outcome filtering.**
- [hume2021-ebp-third-gen]: NCAEP confirms reinforcement, prompting, task analysis, DTT as "evidence-based focused intervention practices" — note: at the **practice-component level**, not the comprehensive EIBI program level.

## Direction
- Positive directional effects on IQ, expressive language, adaptive behavior. Null on autism symptoms and problem behavior.
- Effect magnitudes are large (IQ +15) at heroic intensity (30 hr/wk × 2 yr). At realistic LATAM intensities, effect estimates are unknown.
- Heterogeneity is severe (I²=94% in Yu 2020 for autism symptoms).

# Controversies

## Methodological
- Most "ABA evidence" is single-subject design or non-randomized. The Cochrane review found only **1 RCT** of EIBI in nearly 50 years of practice.
- Comparator floor problem: "treatment as usual" and "eclectic" comparators are heterogeneous and often under-dosed.
- Publication bias and developer-led trials — Lovaas's lab, ESDM developers, JASPER developers all author the foundational trials.
- Project AIM ([sandbank2023-project-aim-update]) finds adverse events under-reported across the field.

## Ethical / neurodiversity critique
- **Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN)** position: ABA's historical and ongoing emphasis on eliminating autistic behaviors (stimming, scripting, lack of eye contact) is harmful — these behaviors are coping mechanisms, not pathologies.
- **[kupferstein2018-aba-ptsd]**: Online survey n=460 reporting PTSD symptom rates 46% higher in ABA-exposed autistic individuals. Methodological caveats severe (cross-sectional, snowball sampling, recall bias, no clinical PTSD diagnosis), but the paper has become the citation anchor for the harm hypothesis. Confidence: **low**, but the question it raises is real and unresolved.
- **Lovaas's historical practices** (electric shocks for non-compliance, conversion-therapy-adjacent applications) are now repudiated by most BCBAs but inform community memory.
- **Compliance and masking**: critics argue ABA trains autistic children to suppress natural behavior, which contributes to long-term mental health harm (autistic burnout, masking-related depression, identity suppression).
- **[mathur2024-aba-neurodiversity]**: BCBAs themselves now publish reform agendas — assent-based teaching, dropping eye-contact and quiet-hands targets, choosing socially valid (to the child, not just the family) goals, partnering with autistic adults in goal selection.

## Current state of debate (2026)
- Mainstream behavior-analytic field has shifted toward naturalistic delivery and is partially absorbing neurodiversity critique ([mathur2024-aba-neurodiversity]).
- Research evidence for high-intensity classical EIBI remains "weak" by Cochrane standards.
- Insurance-driven US ABA market continues to expand, often at lower fidelity than research protocols.
- Long-term outcome studies (10+ years) of ABA-exposed cohorts are essentially absent — the Kupferstein hypothesis cannot be properly tested with current data.

# Cost
- **US**: $40,000–$80,000/year for full EIBI (25–40 hr/wk). Insurance covers most via state mandates; out-of-pocket gap varies.
- **EU**: Variable; UK NHS commissions EIBI rarely; private cost ~£30,000–£60,000/year. Italy, Germany, Spain reimburse partially.
- **LATAM**: Almost entirely private. Centers in México (CDMX, Monterrey), Argentina (BA), Chile (Santiago), Brazil (SP, RJ) charge **USD $20,000–$40,000/year** for partial intensity (10–20 hr/wk). Full 30+ hr/wk EIBI at research fidelity is rare.
- **Peru specifically**: Limited number of private ABA-claiming centers in Lima. BCBA-credentialed providers extremely scarce; most provision is by psychologists with ABA training rather than certified BCBAs. Costs USD $1,500–$3,500/month for partial intensity. See [defensoria2023-peru-autism-barriers], [delacernaluna2024-peru-rebagliati].

# Availability in Peru / LATAM
- Peru: handful of centers in Lima (CASP, CPAL-affiliated, several private practices). No public coverage. Quality variation extreme.
- LATAM private ABA market is growing fastest in MX and BR; CL and AR have established centers; CO emerging.
- BCBA certification pathway requires English-language exam — language barrier limits practitioner pipeline.
- Almost no Spanish-language ABA training materials with research backing; most LATAM clinicians train via English manuals or Pyramid (PECS) materials.

# Tech opportunities
- **Data capture during sessions** is the highest-yield ABA software wedge. Session-based ABCDE (antecedent–behavior–consequence) data is universally collected — Catalyst, Rethink Behavioral Health, CentralReach are entrenched US incumbents. LATAM equivalent is wide open.
- **Parent training apps** delivering ABA-component teaching (prompting, reinforcement schedules, task analysis) — Project ImPACT-style but focused on ABA components. Coach-in-the-loop required for evidence support.
- **Programme automation**: target sequencing, mastery criteria, generalization probes — algorithmically enforceable.
- **VR-augmented ABA**: [floreo2025-cortica-rct-aim] is the first multi-site RCT of VR adjunct to ABA; emerging category.
- **Ethical guardrails as feature**: a product can require justification for goals like "reduce stimming" or "increase eye contact"; can require explicit child-assent tracking; can show parent the neurodiversity counter-evidence at goal-setting time. This is a defensible competitive position for a non-US, neurodiversity-aligned product.
- **Caution**: a wellness/education product cannot claim to be "ABA" without BCBA supervision in regulated jurisdictions. Building the *components* (visual schedules, reinforcement tracking, communication training) without the regulated label is the safer path.

# Evidence
- [reichow2018-eibi-cochrane] — Cochrane EIBI, GRADE low, IQ MD 15.4, adaptive 9.6.
- [rodgers2020-eiba-hta] — UK NIHR HTA: limited, poor-quality evidence; cost-effectiveness uncertain.
- [yu2020-aba-meta] — 14 RCT meta: only expressive language significant.
- [sandbank2020-project-aim] / [sandbank2023-project-aim-update] — Behavioral category cannot pass quality filters.
- [hume2021-ebp-third-gen] — Reinforcement, prompting, DTT confirmed as EBP at component level.
- [kupferstein2018-aba-ptsd] — PTSD-symptom survey, low confidence, high citation salience.
- [mathur2024-aba-neurodiversity] — BCBA-authored reform proposal integrating neurodiversity values.

# Open questions
- Long-term (10+ year) outcomes of ABA-exposed cohorts vs matched controls — essentially zero data.
- Dose-response curve below 25 hr/week — does 10 hr/week deliver proportional benefit, or is the relationship nonlinear?
- Independent (non-Lovaas-lineage) replication of EIBI's classical "best outcome" findings — still outstanding.
- Quantitative test of the masking → mental-health-harm pathway with prospective design.
- Cost-effectiveness in LATAM context — UK NIHR HTA found uncertainty in NHS context; LATAM data nonexistent.
- Whether the documented gains in language/IQ would also accrue to NDBI delivery at lower intensity (early evidence suggests yes — [fuller2020-esdm-meta]).

# Builder takeaways
- **Do not market a wellness/education product as "ABA"** without BCBA supervision and regulatory clearance. Market the components: visual schedules, prompting hierarchies, reinforcement tracking, parent coaching.
- **Honest framing**: "evidence-based for skill acquisition; not a treatment for autism." This matches what [yu2020-aba-meta] and [reichow2018-eibi-cochrane] actually show.
- **Engage the neurodiversity critique**, do not dismiss it. A product positioned for the LATAM and EU market — where neurodiversity acceptance is rising — should treat assent, child-led goal selection, and avoidance of normalization targets as features, not concessions.
- **Data capture is the LATAM ABA wedge**. US incumbents (Catalyst, CentralReach) do not localize. Spanish-first session data + insurer/regulator-compliant reporting + BCBA-supervision workflow is a credible product.
- **Component-based parent training** (prompting, reinforcement, NET) sits in the unregulated wellness space and has an evidence anchor via the NDBI literature.
- **Avoid Lovaas-era branding entirely**. Discrete trial training works for some skills; the imagery and history of 1970s–1990s ABA are liabilities for a 2026 brand.
- **Track adverse events as a first-class data type** ([sandbank2023-project-aim-update]). The category-wide vacuum on adverse-event monitoring is a defensible differentiator and an ethical baseline.
