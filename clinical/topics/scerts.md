# Summary
SCERTS (Social Communication, Emotional Regulation, Transactional Supports; Prizant, Wetherby, Rubin, Laurent, Rydell, 2006) is a comprehensive, transdisciplinary educational model designed primarily for school and classroom delivery. The 2022 systematic review ([yi2022-scerts-review], 6 studies / n=455 across 15+ years post-publication) finds **emerging evidence for social communication outcomes** but cannot produce pooled effect estimates — most studies lack control groups and 2/6 are gray literature. Project AIM 2020/2023 groups SCERTS under "developmental" interventions — the category retains quality-adjusted effects but SCERTS-specific evidence is thin. Despite the limited research base, SCERTS is **widely adopted in school systems** in the US, UK, Spain (Cataluña has formally integrated SCERTS), and Singapore, and its Transactional Supports framework (visual aids, modified language, predictable routines) is the most directly software-friendly component of any major comprehensive model.

# What it is
- **SCERTS** structures intervention around three core domains:
  - **SC — Social Communication**: joint attention, symbol use, conventional means of communication.
  - **ER — Emotional Regulation**: self-regulation, mutual regulation, recovery from dysregulation.
  - **TS — Transactional Supports**: interpersonal supports (partner adjusts), learning supports (visual aids, environmental structure), family/professional supports.
- Cross-cuts three developmental "stages": Social Partner (preverbal), Language Partner (early symbolic), Conversational Partner (symbolic).
- Integrates evidence-based practices from multiple traditions (developmental, behavioral, sensory) within a single framework — explicitly atheoretical/eclectic.

# Origin
- Barry Prizant, Amy Wetherby, Emily Rubin, Amy Laurent, Patrick Rydell — published manual 2006 (Brookes Publishing).
- Builds on Prizant & Wetherby's earlier work in childhood communication disorders and Wetherby's CSBS.
- Designed explicitly to **integrate with classroom delivery** rather than replace it — the only major model with this design intent.

# Target population
- Children, adolescents, and young adults with ASD across the spectrum, ages ~2–22.
- Classroom-age population is the primary use case (school-based delivery).
- Can be adapted for home and community settings.

# Delivery model + intensity
- Embedded in daily classroom and home routines — not a discrete weekly therapy.
- Multidisciplinary team (SLT, OT, teacher, BCBA when present) collaborates around shared SCERTS goals.
- Assessment-Curriculum tool (SAP — SCERTS Assessment Process) drives goal-setting.
- Intensity: continuous environmental embedding + targeted supports across the school day.

# Evidence summary

## Headline effects
- [yi2022-scerts-review] (6 studies, n=455, 2014–2021):
  - Most studies less vulnerable to bias suggested SCERTS effective for promoting social communication.
  - Implementers achieved adequate fidelity through training.
  - Inconsistent or insufficient evidence for other outcomes.
  - **No pooled effect sizes — narrative synthesis only**.
  - 2/6 studies from gray literature (not peer-reviewed).
- [sandbank2023-project-aim-update]: Developmental interventions retain g=0.28 quality-adjusted on social communication; SCERTS specifically not separately analyzed.
- [hume2021-ebp-third-gen]: SCERTS not separately listed as EBP; many SCERTS-aligned focused-intervention practices (visual supports, modeling, prompting, naturalistic intervention) are confirmed EBPs at component level.

## Direction
- Positive directional signal for social communication.
- Insufficient evidence for autism symptoms, language, adaptive behavior, regulation outcomes.
- Adoption in school systems (Cataluña, US districts, UK SEN provision) outpaces published evidence.

# Controversies
- **Six studies in 15+ years** of a model with substantial educational adoption is a striking research-practice gap.
- **No RCTs** of SCERTS as a comprehensive model; existing studies are quasi-experimental, single-subject, or narrative.
- **"Eclectic" framing problem**: SCERTS deliberately integrates multiple traditions, which is clinically pragmatic but makes mechanism / active-ingredient identification impossible.
- **Comparison with TEACCH and JASPER**: SCERTS overlaps substantially with TEACCH (visual supports, structured routines) and JASPER (joint attention, social communication targets) but has weaker independent evidence than either.
- **Implementation fidelity**: SCERTS requires cross-disciplinary team coordination that schools rarely sustain at fidelity.

# Cost
- **US**: SCERTS training ~$1,500–$3,000 per practitioner. School-based implementation cost similar to TEACCH — embedded in standard SPED budgets, low marginal cost per child.
- **EU**: Spain (Cataluña) has the most formal adoption — public-system integration in some autonomous communities. UK has growing adoption in SEN provision.
- **LATAM**: Limited formal adoption. Some private special-education schools in MX, BR, AR reference SCERTS.
- **Peru**: No identified formal SCERTS implementation. Some private practitioners aware of the model.
- Per-child marginal cost in a SCERTS-aligned classroom: USD $5,000–$15,000/year depending on staffing.

# Availability in Peru / LATAM
- Limited availability. Spanish-language SCERTS materials exist (Brookes publishes Spanish editions); training pipeline weak in LATAM.
- Cataluña / Spain is the closest Spanish-language exemplar; their materials and training network are accessible.
- Public-system adoption in LATAM is plausible long-term given SCERTS's school-friendly design and lack of licensing barrier.

# Tech opportunities
- **Transactional Supports library**: visual supports, modified-language scripts, predictable-routine templates, environmental-design checklists. Software can deliver these as a Spanish-first toolkit.
- **SAP (SCERTS Assessment Process) digitization**: the assessment is paper-based and burdensome. A digital SAP that produces auto-generated goal sets, progress dashboards, and team-coordination views is a clear product wedge.
- **Cross-disciplinary coordination**: SCERTS fails operationally when SLP / OT / teacher don't coordinate. A shared-workspace product centered on SCERTS goals and updates is structurally aligned with the model's failure mode.
- **Visual supports + AAC integration**: SCERTS treats AAC as a Transactional Support; a SCERTS-framed AAC product (with visual schedules, modified-language scripts, communication-temptation scripts) integrates communication and broader environmental design. See [aac-symbol-systems].
- **Stage-based developmental tracking**: Social Partner / Language Partner / Conversational Partner stages map to clear app-state UX.
- **Limitations**: do not over-claim SCERTS-specific outcomes (evidence is too thin). Frame around the component evidence — visual supports, parent coaching, AAC introduction.
- **Adoption advantage**: SCERTS branding has higher institutional acceptance than ABA in education contexts. For a public-system / NGO partnership channel, SCERTS is a more politically compatible frame.

# Evidence
- [yi2022-scerts-review] — 6-study systematic review; emerging evidence for social communication; no pooled effect sizes.
- [sandbank2023-project-aim-update] — Developmental category retains quality-adjusted effects.
- [hume2021-ebp-third-gen] — SCERTS-aligned component practices (visual supports, modeling, prompting) confirmed EBPs.

# Open questions
- High-quality RCT of SCERTS as a comprehensive model — outstanding for 20 years.
- Component-decomposition: which SCERTS elements (Transactional Supports, Emotional Regulation curriculum, SAP-driven goal-setting) carry the effect?
- Cataluña public-system implementation — has any data been published on outcomes?
- Cultural adaptation in LATAM contexts.
- SCERTS for adolescents / young adults — most evidence is preschool and early school-age.

# Builder takeaways
- **SCERTS is institutionally-friendly, evidence-thin**. Use the framework and component evidence; don't over-claim SCERTS-specific outcomes.
- **Digital SAP** (assessment + goal-setting + dashboard) is an obvious software wedge — paper SAP is a known burden in SCERTS-implementing schools.
- **Transactional Supports library** in Spanish is high-value: ARASAAC integration, modified-language script generator, predictable-routine templates.
- **Cross-disciplinary workspace**: SCERTS's coordination failure mode is a clear product opportunity. Shared goal tracking across SLP / OT / teacher / parent.
- **School-system distribution channel**: SCERTS's institutional acceptance (Spain, US, UK SEN) gives a softer institutional path than ABA-branded products. Plausible for MINEDU (Peru), SEP (Mexico) special-education partnerships.
- **Honest evidence framing**: "supports social-communication development; integrates evidence-based component practices" — not "evidence-based comprehensive model." The literature does not yet support the latter.
- **Compatible with neurodiversity framing**: SCERTS treats emotional regulation as legitimate (not as deficit) and partner adjustment as central — aligns with neurodiversity values better than ABA branding.
