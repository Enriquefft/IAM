# Summary
Ayres Sensory Integration (ASI) is **the most contested major autism intervention**. Two reviews of similar literature reach **opposite conclusions**: [schoen2019-asi-review] (3 ASI-fidelity RCTs/non-RCTs, n=96) reports ASI "meets criteria for an evidence-based practice for children 4–12 with autism" with Goal Attainment Scaling (GAS) Cohen's d up to 1.20; [lang2012-si-review] (25 broader sensory integration therapy studies, n=217) reports **56% of studies showed no benefit** and concludes "no consistently positive effect." The disagreement resolves on **definitional fidelity**: Ayres-fidelity ASI (clinic, suspended/vestibular equipment, individualized GAS goals, certified Ayres-trained OT) shows positive evidence on individualized goals; broader "sensory-based" interventions (weighted vests, brushing, sensory diets) lack evidence. The honest position: ASI has **emerging evidence on individualized goals only**, does not reduce autism core symptoms, and broader sensory-based interventions commonly delivered in LATAM private OT practice are **not evidence-based**.

# What it is
- **Ayres SI (ASI)** = a structured occupational therapy approach developed by A. Jean Ayres (1970s) targeting children with sensory processing differences. Uses suspended equipment (swings, hammocks, scooter boards), tactile media, and adaptive challenges in a clinic setting.
- Hypothesized mechanism: providing controlled vestibular, proprioceptive, and tactile input enables CNS to better organize sensory information, supporting praxis (motor planning) and adaptive responses.
- **"Sensory-based" interventions** (broader category): weighted vests, brushing protocols (Wilbarger), sensory diets, joint compressions, deep-pressure tools, oral-motor input. These are **not** Ayres SI.
- The category-mixing problem is the dominant interpretive issue in the literature.

# Origin
- A. Jean Ayres (USC OT, 1972, *Sensory Integration and the Child*).
- Developed primarily for children with learning disabilities and motor planning difficulties; applied to autism from the 1980s onward.
- Now governed by the Ayres Sensory Integration Fidelity Measure (ASIFM) — a 10-element checklist for protocol adherence.

# Target population
- Originally children 4–12 with sensory processing and praxis difficulties.
- Applied across autism spectrum, especially children with significant sensory differences.
- Most evidence in cognitively able children (IQ > 65); less data in profound autism.

# Delivery model + intensity
- 1:1 OT in a clinic with specific equipment (suspended swings, ball pits, climbing structures, tactile bins).
- Recommended dose: 1–2 hr/week × 6+ months for the GAS-supporting evidence.
- Certified Ayres-trained OT preferred; ASIFM fidelity measurement available.

# Evidence summary

## Headline effects (ASI-fidelity)
- [schoen2019-asi-review] (3 included studies, n=96):
  - Schaaf 2014 RCT: GAS Cohen's d = **1.20**, caregiver assistance d = 0.9, social participation d = 0.7.
  - Pfeiffer 2011 RCT: GAS d = 0.36 (teacher) and 0.13 (parent); autism mannerisms d = 0.13.
  - Iwanaga 2014 (non-RCT): average d = 0.23 (below 0.25 threshold).
  - Schaaf met 100% CEC quality criteria; Pfeiffer 85%; Iwanaga ~50%.
- [schaaf2018-asi-systematic-review] (5 studies, AOTA-published): "Strong evidence supports use of ASI for autism on individually-established goals." Effect sizes not formally pooled.

## Headline effects (broader sensory-based)
- [lang2012-si-review] (25 studies, n=217):
  - 3/25 (12%) positive; 8/25 (32%) mixed; **14/25 (56%) no benefit**.
  - "Sensory integration therapy had no consistently positive effect."
- Weighted vest single-subject and weighted blanket trials ([lin2017-weighted-vest-asd-review], [gringras2014-weighted-blanket-rct], [gee2020-weighted-blanket-single-subject]): null on sleep and behavioral outcomes.
- [silver2019-irlen-systematic-review]: Irlen lenses for visual sensitivity — null evidence.

## Direction
- **Ayres-fidelity ASI** on individualized GAS goals: positive (d=0.36–1.20).
- **Broader sensory-based interventions**: null or inconsistent.
- Autism core symptoms: NS even in ASI-fidelity studies.
- Functional outcomes (caregiver assistance, social participation in Schaaf 2014): positive but single-trial.

# Controversies

## Definitional
- The ASI-positive literature requires Ayres protocol fidelity (ASIFM); the SI-null literature mixes Ayres fidelity with weighted vests, brushing, sensory diets, and other "sensory-based" approaches.
- AOTA endorses ASI; AAP (American Academy of Pediatrics, 2012 policy statement) cautioned against "sensory integration therapy" and recommended OT focus on functional skill-building rather than sensory-only interventions.

## Methodological
- GAS (Goal Attainment Scaling) is calibrated to expectation — vulnerable to inflation. Strongest ASI effects (d=1.20) are GAS-based.
- Small samples (n<100 for the ASI-fidelity literature combined).
- Outcome assessor blinding inconsistent.
- "Treatment as usual" comparators heterogeneous.

## Practice gap
- Most OT delivered to autistic children in LATAM private practice is **not** ASIFM-fidelity. Common delivery includes weighted vests, brushing, sensory diets, oral-motor protocols — i.e., the Lang category, not the Schoen category.
- Parents report substantial out-of-pocket spending on "sensory" interventions whose evidence base does not match what they receive.

## Sensory differences as autism core feature
- DSM-5/5-TR added sensory differences as a B4 diagnostic criterion ([green2016-sensory-dsm5]). This legitimizes sensory-focused intervention as targeting a core autism feature, not just a comorbidity.
- But: addressing sensory features therapeutically is a different question from whether SI/ASI interventions specifically work.

# Cost
- **US**: $80–$200/session for OT-delivered ASI; insurance coverage varies. Annual cost USD $4,000–$15,000 for typical 1–2 sessions/week.
- **EU**: Variable; UK NHS rarely commissions; private similar to US.
- **LATAM**: USD $30–$80/session in private practice. Annual cost USD $1,500–$5,000. **OT is the most-accessed therapy in LATAM private autism care** — often more accessible than SLP or psychology.
- **Peru**: Lima OT clinics common (e.g., CASP, CPAL, multiple private practices). Prices USD $40–$100/session. Sensory-themed OT widely advertised. ASIFM-certified providers extremely scarce. See [defensoria2023-peru-autism-barriers].

# Availability in Peru / LATAM
- OT is the most-accessed autism therapy in LATAM private practice.
- ASIFM-certified providers very few. Most OTs received generalist training with sensory-integration coursework but not full Ayres certification.
- Parents and clinicians often use "integración sensorial" and "terapia sensorial" interchangeably, blurring the ASI/sensory-based distinction critical to the evidence interpretation.
- Equipment (swings, vestibular gear) is present in well-resourced private clinics; absent in public-system OT.

# Tech opportunities
- **Software has limited replacement potential for ASI itself** — the intervention's mechanism depends on physical vestibular/proprioceptive input through specialized equipment. Software cannot deliver suspended-swing input.
- **Augmentation paths**:
  - **Sensory profile assessment**: digital sensory profile questionnaires (Dunn Sensory Profile 2, SPM-2) for parent/teacher/clinician rating; longitudinal tracking; auto-generated environmental recommendations.
  - **Sensory diary**: parent-tracked sensory triggers, regulation strategies tried, outcomes — feeds dashboard for OT collaboration.
  - **Wearable HRV / EDA integration**: regulation state monitoring tied to sensory environment context. See [hardware-landscape], [papoutsi2022-asd-wearables-review], [khoury2025-wearable-asd-stress-systematic].
  - **Environmental design library**: photo + text guides for low-arousal home/classroom setup, sensory-friendly outing planning, transition supports.
  - **Honest "sensory toolkit" content**: present the evidence on weighted blankets ([gringras2014-weighted-blanket-rct] null), noise-cancelling headphones ([pfeiffer2019-noise-headphones-asd]), fidget tools ([graziano2018-fidget-spinner-adhd]) so parents can choose informed.
- **Limitations**:
  - Software cannot claim to deliver ASI.
  - Weighted blankets, brushing protocols, sensory diets — **do not endorse without evidence flag**. Lang 2012 + AAP 2012 are the cautionary anchors.
  - Honest framing: "support sensory regulation and environmental adaptation" — not "treat sensory processing disorder."

# Evidence
- [schoen2019-asi-review] — 3 ASI-fidelity studies; positive on GAS.
- [schaaf2018-asi-systematic-review] — AOTA-published; supports ASI for individualized goals.
- [lang2012-si-review] — Broader SI null; 56% no benefit.
- [silver2019-irlen-systematic-review] — Irlen lenses null.
- [gringras2014-weighted-blanket-rct] — Weighted blanket null on sleep.
- [gee2020-weighted-blanket-single-subject] — Weighted blanket single-subject; mixed.
- [lin2017-weighted-vest-asd-review] — Weighted vest review; null.
- [pfeiffer2019-noise-headphones-asd] — Noise headphones; some benefit.
- [green2016-sensory-dsm5] — DSM-5 sensory criterion legitimizing sensory features.
- [morimoto2021-sensory-asd] — Sensory differences as core autism feature.

# Open questions
- Independent (non-Schaaf-lineage) replication of GAS d=1.20 magnitudes — outstanding.
- Component decomposition of ASI: which equipment / which input modality is the active ingredient?
- Sensory diet interventions: well-implemented sensory-diet RCT is missing.
- Wearable-feedback-informed environmental adaptation — early signals; no autism-specific outcome RCTs.
- Cultural validity of sensory profile questionnaires in LATAM contexts.

# Builder takeaways
- **Software cannot replace ASI** — physical vestibular/proprioceptive input is the mechanism. Augmentation is the realistic frame.
- **Sensory profile + sensory diary + environmental design** are the defensible software components. Pair with OT collaboration workflow if available.
- **Wearable integration** (HRV, EDA) is an emerging differentiator — physiological regulation data tied to sensory context is novel and software-tractable.
- **Be honest about non-evidence-based "sensory" tools** (weighted blankets, brushing, vestibular swings without ASI fidelity). Educate rather than endorse.
- **Avoid "treats sensory processing disorder" claims**. SPD is not a DSM diagnosis; "sensory processing differences" or "sensory features" are the supportable framings.
- **LATAM specific**: OT is the most-accessed therapy; a parent-OT collaboration tool (sensory diary, profile, goals tracking) has natural distribution. Bilingual sensory profile questionnaires are a content-only wedge.
- **Educate on ASI vs. broader SI** in onboarding content. Parents should know which they're paying for and what evidence supports each.
