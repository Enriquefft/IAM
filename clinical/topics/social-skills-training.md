# Summary
Social-skills training (SST) for autistic individuals splits into three evidentiary tiers. **Tier 1 (RCT-supported)**: UCLA PEERS — 14–16-week manualized curriculum for adolescents and young adults with caregiver coaching. [laugeson2015-peers-youngadults] (n=22 RCT) and additional Laugeson lab trials show significant gains on SSRS, social knowledge (TASSK), and SRS, with most gains persisting at 16-week follow-up. **Tier 2 (contested EBP)**: Social Stories — [qi2018-social-stories-review] (22 single-case studies) reaches **opposite conclusions depending on analytical method** — visual analysis fails the WWC EBP threshold, nonoverlap indices pass it. **Tier 3 (practice convention with no RCT)**: Comic Strip Conversations and most school-based "social skills groups" have minimal controlled evidence. Across all SST modalities, **generalization beyond the training context is the dominant unsolved problem**. Software has high natural fit — SST is structured, manualized, role-play-heavy, and homework-driven — but pure-software SST without human practice partners is unevidenced.

# Programmes covered

## PEERS (Program for the Education and Enrichment of Relational Skills, UCLA)
- **Origin**: Elizabeth Laugeson, UCLA, 2009. Adolescent version (PEERS for Adolescents) and young-adult version (PEERS for Young Adults) widely disseminated.
- **Target population**: cognitively able autistic adolescents (13–17) and young adults (18–24); IQ > 70 typical.
- **Mechanism**: 14–16 weekly 90-min group sessions. Concrete rules + role-play + caregiver-coached homework (week-by-week assignments tracking real-world social engagement: get-togethers, conversations, handling rejection, dating skills).
- **Evidence**: [laugeson2015-peers-youngadults] n=22 RCT — significant gains on overall social skills (SSRS), social engagement frequency, social skills knowledge (TASSK), autism symptoms (SRS); gains persisted at 16-week follow-up. Multiple additional Laugeson-lab trials replicate similar findings; some independent replications.
- **Effect sizes**: significant pre-post on multiple measures; effect-size magnitudes inconsistently reported.
- **Licensing**: PEERS manual published; certified PEERS providers via UCLA training. International expansion ongoing.

## Social Stories (Carol Gray, 1991)
- **Origin**: Carol Gray, Jenison MI public schools, 1991. Defined by 10 specific structural criteria (descriptive, perspective, directive, affirmative, control, cooperative sentences in defined ratios + visual format + explicit topic + read 1:1).
- **Target population**: school-age autistic children across cognitive levels.
- **Mechanism**: short narrative (typically 1–2 pages, illustrated) describing a social situation, relevant social cues, and appropriate responses. Read with the child before the situation occurs.
- **Evidence**: [qi2018-social-stories-review] — 22 single-case-design studies. **Verdict by visual analysis: NOT evidence-based practice (per WWC standards). Verdict by nonoverlap indices: WOULD qualify as EBP.** Most studies did not follow Gray's 10 criteria — fidelity is the gap.
- **Effect sizes**: by nonoverlap (PND/IRD): positive overall. By visual analysis (WWC preference): mixed; does not meet 5-3-20 criteria.
- **Licensing**: Carol Gray's framework is published; commercial Social Story books and apps abundant; few enforce the 10 criteria.

## Comic Strip Conversations (Carol Gray)
- **Mechanism**: drawing-based conversation analysis using stick figures, speech bubbles, and color-coded emotion. Used to deconstruct past or upcoming social situations.
- **Evidence**: practice convention with no controlled trial evidence. Not separately listed as EBP in [hume2021-ebp-third-gen].
- **Status**: widely used in schools; evidence base essentially absent.

## School-based social skills groups
- **Mechanism**: typically 30–60 min group sessions, mixed-format (video modeling, role-play, peer interaction practice). Highly variable.
- **Evidence**: video modeling and modeling are confirmed EBPs at component level ([hume2021-ebp-third-gen]). Comprehensive school-based "social skills group" formats vary too widely for a clean evidence base.
- **Generalization problem**: the central critique — gains in the group setting do not reliably transfer to natural social contexts.

## Computer / VR / robot-mediated SST
- **FaceSay**: avatar-based emotion recognition app. [hopkins2011-facesay-rct] n=49 RCT — improved face/emotion recognition and playground interaction.
- **VR social skills**: see [yang2025-vr-autism-systematic-review] — emerging evidence; small samples; novelty effects unresolved.
- **Robot-mediated SST**: see [kouroupa2022-social-robots-meta-analysis] — moderate effects in research contexts; ecological validity unclear.

# What they share
- Explicit teaching of social conventions that neurotypical peers acquire implicitly.
- Role-play / practice in low-stakes context.
- Generalization-to-natural-context as the core unsolved problem.
- Caregiver / peer / teacher involvement strengthens generalization (PEERS's caregiver coaching is the empirical anchor).

# Evidence summary

## Headline effects
- PEERS young adults ([laugeson2015-peers-youngadults]): SSRS, TASSK, SRS, social engagement frequency — all significant pre-post; gains sustained at 16 weeks.
- Social Stories ([qi2018-social-stories-review]): contested EBP — methodology-dependent verdict.
- Video modeling ([hume2021-ebp-third-gen]): confirmed EBP at component level.
- FaceSay ([hopkins2011-facesay-rct]): improved face/emotion recognition + playground interaction.

## Direction
- PEERS: reliable gains within program scope; generalization to broader social functioning incomplete.
- Social Stories: positive directional signal but contingent on Gray-fidelity construction and EBP threshold method.
- Generalization beyond training context: weak across all modalities.
- Adult / young-adult outcomes (PEERS): strongest evidence cluster for autism social skills work in this age band.

# Controversies
- **Neurodiversity critique of SST in general**: teaching autistic people to mask / camouflage social differences may contribute to long-term mental health harm (autistic burnout, identity suppression, depression). Critics argue SST should focus on **mutual understanding** (autistic + neurotypical bidirectional adaptation) rather than autistic-side-only adjustment. Double-empathy literature ([loomes2017-mf-ratio]'s underlying framework + Damian Milton's work) underpins this critique.
- **Generalization gap**: across SST formats, gains in the training context rarely transfer to natural social contexts without explicit generalization programming.
- **Social Stories' methodological dispute**: the WWC-vs-nonoverlap discrepancy in [qi2018-social-stories-review] illustrates how EBP designation can depend on analytical conventions.
- **PEERS's caregiver requirement** may exclude independent autistic adults — a known limitation.
- **Cognitive-ability requirement**: most SST evidence is in cognitively able autistic individuals (IQ > 70); evidence for higher-support-needs populations is weaker.

# Cost
- **PEERS**:
  - **US**: PEERS-certified provider USD $1,000–$3,000 for the 16-week programme. Some university clinics offer at low cost.
  - **EU**: Available in UK, Spain, Germany; pricing similar.
  - **LATAM**: Certified providers in MX, BR, CL; limited. Cost USD $500–$2,000 typical.
  - **Peru**: Limited identified PEERS-certified providers as of 2026.
- **Social Stories**: DIY (free) or USD $0–$100 in apps. Carol Gray Social Stories books $15–$30.
- **School-based SST groups**: often included in IEP services; private USD $50–$150/session.
- **VR / robot SST**: research-context only; commercial products emerging (Floreo VR USD $30/month, see [floreo2025-cortica-rct-aim], [ravindran2019-floreo-joint-attention-pilot]).

# Availability in Peru / LATAM
- **PEERS**: limited LATAM presence; UCLA expanded Spanish-language certification in late 2010s; few certified providers per LATAM country.
- **Social Stories**: widely used informally by teachers; few apps in Spanish; AI-generated Spanish Social Stories an obvious gap.
- **VR-based SST**: minimal LATAM availability; cost barrier (Quest headsets) significant.
- **Robot-based SST**: research-only; no commercial deployment in LATAM identified.

# Tech opportunities

## High-leverage software wedges

- **AI-generated Social Stories per Gray's 10 criteria**: the literature explicitly identifies fidelity as the gap ([qi2018-social-stories-review]). An LLM that constructs Social Stories conforming to Gray's specific sentence-type ratios, written for a specific child + situation, in Spanish + English, is a clear fidelity-fixing wedge. License-cleared illustrations (ARASAAC + custom AI illustration) handle the visual layer.
- **PEERS homework tracker + caregiver-coaching tools**: PEERS's evidence depends on weekly homework + caregiver involvement. Software for tracking get-togethers, role-play prompts, post-session caregiver debrief — natural extension.
- **Video modeling library**: short, authentic, autistic-led videos for specific social situations. Confirmed EBP at component level ([hume2021-ebp-third-gen]).
- **Conversation script generator**: AI-generated scripts for specific upcoming situations (job interview, doctor visit, asking out a peer) personalized to the user's profile.
- **Get-togethers / dating planner** (PEERS-aligned): plan, practice, debrief structure for real-world social attempts.
- **Generalization programming**: explicit prompts, in-the-moment cue cards (smartphone-delivered), post-event debrief — all software-tractable mechanisms for closing the generalization gap.

## Lower-confidence opportunities

- **VR social skills**: emerging evidence; novelty effect risk; cost-of-hardware barrier in LATAM.
- **Robot-mediated**: research-only; not LATAM-deployable at scale.
- **Pure AI-chatbot social practice**: no autism-specific RCT evidence; ethical concerns documented in adjacent LLM literature ([wohn2026-disclosure-stereotypes-llm], [jang2024-llm-autistic-workers-chi], [iftikhar2025-llm-mental-health-ethics]). Risk of reinforcing masking patterns.

## Honest framing
- Pure-software SST without human practice partners is unevidenced.
- Generalization to natural contexts is the unresolved problem — software should be designed with explicit generalization programming.
- Neurodiversity-aware SST: focus on bidirectional understanding (teach autistic person AND prepare neurotypical environment) rather than autistic-side masking.

# Open questions
- Generalization-programming protocols that demonstrably close the training-to-natural-context gap.
- PEERS for higher-support-needs populations (lower IQ, minimally verbal).
- AI-generated Social Stories: do they replicate human-authored Social Stories' (contested) effects?
- Long-term (2+ year) outcomes of SST programmes — limited data across modalities.
- Neurodiversity-aligned SST design: empirical comparison of autistic-focused vs. bidirectional / environment-side approaches.
- LATAM-context SST efficacy — minimal published data.

# Builder takeaways
- **AI-generated Social Stories per Gray's 10 criteria** is a textbook software-fixes-fidelity wedge. The literature explicitly identifies fidelity as the gap; software can guarantee what human authors cannot enforce at scale.
- **PEERS is the highest-evidence anchor** for adolescent / young-adult social skills work. License + certification dependent — building a PEERS-augmenting tool (homework tracking, caregiver coaching) is plausible; building a "PEERS replacement" is not.
- **Generalization programming is the unsolved problem and the hardest software wedge**. JIT smartphone cue cards, post-event debrief, in-context prompts — all defensible features.
- **Neurodiversity-aware framing**: focus on mutual understanding and environmental adaptation, not autistic-side masking. The "double empathy" framing is increasingly mainstream and provides ethical positioning.
- **Spanish-first SST content** is a gap. PEERS curriculum has Spanish translation but limited certified-provider network; Social Stories in Spanish are scarce as quality content.
- **Adult / young-adult market is underserved**. Most autism software targets young children; PEERS-aligned adult tools (job-interview practice, dating skills, conflict resolution) have evidence support and limited competition.
- **Video modeling library** is low-build-cost, high-evidence-anchor. Authentic autistic-led videos for specific social situations have direct EBP support.
- **Avoid masking-promoting framing**. Marketing that promises "fitting in" or "passing as neurotypical" carries documented harm risk and ethical liability.
- **Caregiver-mediation pattern transfers**: PEERS's caregiver coaching aligns with parent-mediated NDBI literature ([parent-mediated-interventions]). Cross-product caregiver dashboard infrastructure can serve both spaces.
