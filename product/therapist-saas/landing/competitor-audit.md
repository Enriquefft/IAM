# Competitor Landing Page Audit

Covers 7 landing pages fetched 2026-05-02. Audit framework per competitor: hero headline + subhead (verbatim), primary CTA, hero visual, social proof, section order, pricing visibility, one strength, one failure for LATAM Spanish TEA therapist persona.

**Note on TheraNest:** theranest.com 301-redirects to ensorahealth.com/product/mental-health/ — TheraNest is now a product under Ensora Health. Audited at redirect destination. Original brand effectively dead as a standalone landing.

**Note on Theralytics:** theralytics.com 301-redirects to theralytics.net — audited at redirect destination, content appears canonical.

## 1. SimplePractice — simplepractice.com

**Original language:** English

**Hero headline:** The page leads with a promotional banner as the dominant above-fold element rather than a classic H1 hero: *"Get free credentialing when you sign up for SimplePractice"* / *"Get paneled with up to two payers when you sign up for SimplePractice."* The underlying product H1 is: *"Intuitive tools to help you thrive"* (revealed below the specialty carousel).

**Hero subhead:** *"Our EHR software includes integrated telehealth, a robust template library, and helpful insurance tools, making it easy for you to manage your practice while caring for clients."* (associated with the Therapists specialty tab, not a global subhead)

**Primary CTA:** "Start free trial" — top-right header + hero section button (dual placement). Secondary: "Claim this offer" (credentialing promo, also header + hero).

**Hero visual:** Photo — woman at desk on a telehealth call. No product UI screenshot above the fold.

**Social proof:** Quantitative stat ("Over 20M clients and 250k practitioners trust the SimplePractice platform") placed mid-page below features. Partner/association logos (APA, ASHA, ACA, CAMFT, AAMFT) in a dedicated section further down. No testimonial quotes above the fold.

**Section order (top to bottom):**
1. Header / nav
2. Promotional credentialing offer banner (dominant above-fold)
3. Specialty carousel — "Who we're for" (Therapists, SLPs, OTs, Psychiatrists)
4. Feature section: "Streamline admin tasks" / "Improve client care" / "Manage & grow your practice"
5. Social proof + customer stories ("Over 20M clients…")
6. Association partner logos
7. Footer (email signup, nav links, HIPAA / HITRUST / PCI compliance badges)

**Pricing visibility:** Hidden — linked from nav but not displayed on landing. No prices on page.

**What they do well:** The specialty carousel ("Who we're for") immediately signals relevance to SLPs and OTs — segments that are underserved by generic EHRs — without fragmenting the page into separate products.

**What fails for LATAM Spanish TEA therapist persona:** The credentialing promo (insurance panel enrollment) is the dominant above-fold message — irrelevant for cash-pay LATAM practice where no insurance billing exists, and the entire page is English-only with USD implicit pricing, leaving the LATAM buyer with zero localization signal.

## 2. TheraNest (now Ensora Health) — ensorahealth.com/product/mental-health/

**Original language:** English

**Hero headline:** *"Mental Health EHR"*

**Hero subhead:** *"Feeling bogged down in administrative tasks? TheraNest by Ensora Health is an EHR and practice management solution that makes billing, scheduling, and notes a breeze – giving you precious time back in your day."*

**Primary CTA:** "Start a free trial" + "Request a demo" — both in the hero section (side-by-side buttons). No prominent header CTA noted separately.

**Hero visual:** Photograph — professional woman working on a laptop in an office. No product UI screenshot.

**Social proof:** Customer testimonials with name and title, placed mid-page below feature section. Association logos (ACA, APA) further down. No above-fold social proof.

**Section order (top to bottom):**
1. Navigation header with product menu (multi-product Ensora umbrella)
2. Hero section (headline, subhead, two CTAs, photo)
3. Core features: AI Session Assistant, Automated billing, Client Portal, Reporting, Scheduling
4. Video testimonial section ("Making your days a little bit easier")
5. Scalability section ("Scalable and secure for growing organizations")
6. Comparison section ("Built for solo and small group mental health practices")
7. RCM (Revenue Cycle Management) upsell
8. Association logos (ACA, APA)
9. Customer testimonials carousel
10. Pricing overview with link to detailed pricing page
11. Footer

**Pricing visibility:** Partially visible — a pricing overview section exists on the landing with a link to a dedicated pricing page. Specific prices not displayed inline.

**What they do well:** The subhead directly names the pain ("Feeling bogged down in administrative tasks?") and maps it to the solution in a single sentence — the clearest pain-to-relief arc of any competitor in this set.

**What fails for LATAM Spanish TEA therapist persona:** Acquired by Ensora Health (a US enterprise health-tech rollup), the product is now nested inside a multi-product corporate umbrella that signals enterprise scale, not solo-practice affordability; the RCM upsell section (US insurance billing automation) is actively alienating to a cash-pay LATAM buyer.

## 3. CentralReach — centralreach.com

**Original language:** English

**Hero headline:** *"AI-Powered EMR platform for Autism and IDD Care"*

**Hero subhead:** *"The ABA practice management software trusted by 4,000 ABA & Multi-D practices and over 200,000 professionals"*

**Social proof:** Customer logo carousel (20+ named organizations) placed immediately below the hero — the highest placement of any competitor audited. Stat embedded in subhead ("4,000 ABA practices, 200,000+ professionals").

**Section order (top to bottom):**

8. AI philosophy section ("AI. Done Responsibly")
9. Final CTA section

**Pricing visibility:** Not visible. No pricing anywhere on landing. Fully behind consultation wall.

**What they do well:** Placing the enterprise customer logo carousel immediately below the hero compresses the "trust establishment" moment to 2 seconds of scroll — the most efficient social-proof placement of any competitor here.

**What fails for LATAM Spanish TEA therapist persona:** Every trust signal is enterprise-scale (20+ large US ABA organizations, 200,000+ professionals) — a solo LATAM psychologist sees a platform built for US ABA clinics with dozens of staff, not for a one-woman private practice in Lima or Bogotá; the consultation-gated CTA also has zero conversion path for a solo buyer who wants to try before committing.

## 5 Wedge Insights

### Wedge 1: No competitor shows local-currency pricing above the fold — or anywhere on the landing

Every US competitor hides pricing entirely (SimplePractice, CentralReach) or gates it behind a demo call. Theralytics is the sole exception and shows USD pricing that prices LATAM buyers out on the first read ($200–$300/month minimum). Doctoralia and iClinic also hide pricing. **No competitor shows PEN, MXN, COP, ARS, or CLP on any landing page.** i-am.clinic should display the local-currency equivalent above the fold: "S/75/mes · MX$350/mes · COP$80,000/mes" with a one-line note that the USD 20/mo solo price converts to these figures. This is not just a conversion tactic — it signals "built for you, not translated for you."

### Wedge 2: No competitor names the Word/Excel/WhatsApp stack as the enemy

Every US competitor positions against other SaaS tools ("all-in-one," "replace your current EHR"). None acknowledge that the real competitor for LATAM solo therapists is a Word document, an Excel sheet, and a personal WhatsApp account. TheraNest comes closest with "Feeling bogged down in administrative tasks?" but then pivots immediately to EHR features. **i-am.clinic's landing can name the actual stack verbatim** — "¿Todavía gestionas tus pacientes con Word, Excel y WhatsApp?" — and thereby immediately signal category fluency that US incumbents cannot credibly claim. This is a direct-response positioning move none of the 7 have executed.

### Wedge 3: No competitor quantifies time recovered in the hero

Social proof on every competitor landing is either institutional (logo carousels, award badges) or anecdotal (testimonial quotes). Zero competitors show a concrete time-recovery number above the fold. The workflows.md audit quantifies that the Word/Excel/WhatsApp stack costs 30–50% of a therapist's work week on non-clinical tasks — a figure no US competitor could claim because they have not done this research for LATAM solo therapists. **i-am.clinic can lead with a specific, sourced number**: "Recupera 6+ horas a la semana que hoy gastas en reagendar, notas y cobranza." This is more precise and more credible than any social proof any competitor currently shows, because it maps to a specific workflow pain (the E29 interview; the wedge ranking in workflows.md).

### Wedge 4: TEA-specific competitors are ABA-clinic-scale and US-insurance-dependent; none speaks to the LATAM outpatient psychologist

CentralReach and Theralytics are the only two competitors that specialize in autism/TEA. Both are built around US ABA clinic economics: CPT billing codes, insurance claim submission, BCBA-credentialed staff, 10-client minimum billing floors, enterprise logo social proof. Neither has any relevance to a cash-pay fonoaudiologa in Bogotá or a solo psicóloga in Lima who has never filed an insurance claim. **i-am.clinic can claim the entire Spanish-speaking TEA therapist segment by default** — there is no direct competitor in that intersection. The landing should name the specialization explicitly: "Para psicólogas, fonoaudiólogas y terapeutas ocupacionales que atienden TEA en consulta privada." This is an uncontested positioning statement.

### Wedge 5: No competitor uses the parent-facing output as a B2B conversion hook

Every competitor's social proof targets the therapist-as-operator (efficiency, billing, scheduling). None use the parent-facing output — the weekly PDF report, the structured progress note — as a conversion argument directed at the therapist. Yet in the workflows.md evidence, therapist guilt about not sending parent summaries is a documented pain ("registro es solo para ella"). **i-am.clinic's landing can show the parent-facing PDF as the hero artifact**: a mock "Resumen semanal de Mateo — semana del 28 de abril" with filled SOAP fields and a plain-language parent narrative. This positions the product as something that makes the therapist look professional to families — an emotional benefit (professional legitimacy, reduced guilt) that no competitor has surfaced, and which is directly observable by the buyer in the UI screenshot.

## Hero Pattern Recommendation

### Closest right structure: Theralytics

Theralytics is the nearest structural match for i-am.clinic's landing, for three reasons:

1. **Product UI screenshot in the hero** — the only US competitor to show actual software in the above-fold area, which immediately concretizes the product for a buyer who has never seen a purpose-built SaaS for their workflow.
2. **Award/credential social proof immediately below hero** — tight compression of trust establishment, important for an unknown brand entering a market where Word/Excel is the status quo.
3. **Pricing visible on the landing** — creates a self-serve evaluation path for solo buyers who will never book a demo call with an unknown vendor.

### 3 specific changes to adapt Theralytics' structure for i-am.clinic

**Change 1: Replace the product-category headline with a pain-named headline in Spanish.**
Theralytics leads with *"Leading ABA Practice Management, Billing and Data Collection Software"* — a category claim. For i-am.clinic, the hero H1 should name the pain in the buyer's own vocabulary, not the product category. Proposed structure: a two-line headline where line 1 names the stack ("¿Todavía gestionas tus pacientes TEA con Word, Excel y WhatsApp?") and line 2 is the relief ("i-am.clinic lo reemplaza en un solo lugar"). The subhead carries the time-recovery number ("Recupera 6+ horas a la semana en reagendar, notas y reportes para padres").

**Change 2: Replace the award-badge row with a local-currency price block immediately below the hero.**
Theralytics' award badges (Capterra, G2) signal validation in the US SaaS evaluation context. A LATAM solo therapist does not consult Capterra before purchasing. The trust signal that converts this buyer is price transparency + no-lock-in: show "S/75/mes · MX$350/mes · COP$80,000/mes — cancela cuando quieras" in the first content section below the hero. Pairs with a "Prueba 30 días gratis — sin tarjeta" CTA. This collapses the trust gap faster than any logo carousel.

**Change 3: Make the hero UI screenshot the parent-facing PDF, not the scheduling dashboard.**
Theralytics shows a scheduling/data-collection UI — the operational back-end. i-am.clinic's hero screenshot should show the parent-facing weekly summary PDF ("Resumen semanal de Mateo") because: (a) it is immediately legible to any therapist without product training, (b) it surfaces the emotional benefit (professional output, reduced guilt) rather than the operational benefit (fewer clicks), and (c) it differentiates from every US competitor whose hero screenshots show calendars or billing grids. The scheduling UI can appear in a features section one scroll below — the parent PDF should be the artifact that earns the click.
